## What Belgrade actually offers in orthopaedics

Serbia has a strong orthopaedic ecosystem, driven largely by heavy domestic demand. In mid-2025 there were tens of thousands of people on hip and knee endoprosthesis waiting lists, with nearly half of them in Belgrade institutions. That figure is worth reading carefully: it is a measure of domestic patients waiting for treatment, not of foreign-patient volume. It tells you the country has surgical capacity and routine experience with these procedures — but it is not evidence that Belgrade is a magnet for international joint replacement.

Against that backdrop, private Belgrade orthopaedic hospitals do market hip and knee replacement, along with sports orthopaedics, to international patients. The language you will see emphasises minimally-invasive techniques and "one-day surgery" pathways. These descriptions are indicative as of 2025, and they are also where a prospective patient should slow down rather than speed up.

## Hip and knee replacement is major surgery

Hip and knee replacement are established procedures with generally good outcomes. They are also major surgery, with real recovery and rehabilitation timelines measured in weeks to months, and the standard surgical and anaesthetic risks that come with any operation of this size. The "one-day surgery" framing refers to how long you spend admitted, not to how long it takes to recover. Do not let the marketing imply that the recovery is trivial — it isn't.

Rehabilitation in particular is substantial. Serbian rehabilitation spas offer post-operative programmes, and the durations described are telling: roughly four to six weeks after a knee replacement, and anywhere from about four weeks to three months after a hip. Those are indicative figures as of 2026, but they sketch the real shape of the commitment. You are not booking a procedure and a flight home a few days later; you are booking a procedure and then a meaningful stretch of recovery somewhere.

## The travel-and-recovery problem

This is where the orthopaedic-specific caution becomes sharp. Major joint surgery, a long rehab period, and the deep-vein-thrombosis risk that comes with flying all stack on top of one another. Flying too soon after this kind of surgery is its own hazard, and the temptation to compress the trip — operation, a short stay, then home — runs directly against what the recovery actually demands.

There is also the question of continuity of care. If a complication appears after you have flown home, the surgeon who operated is now in another country. Arranging follow-up, managing rehab from a distance, and knowing who handles problems are all things to settle before you travel, not after. We go into this in more detail in our guide to [medical tourism risks and aftercare](/medical-tourism/medical-tourism-risks-and-aftercare), and it is worth reading alongside the practical side of [staying safe and healthy in Belgrade](/plan-your-trip/staying-safe). The point is not to discourage anyone — it is that the planning around recovery matters as much as the choice of surgeon.

## The honest limits: cardiology, oncology, bariatric

This is the part that marketing sites tend to leave out, and it is the most useful thing on this page. For several categories of serious-disease care, Serbia is often simply not the destination — and in some cases Serbian patients themselves travel abroad.

### Cardiology

Cardiology is named in Serbian medical-tourism promotion, but there is no strong independent evidence that foreign patients choose Belgrade specifically for cardiology over more established centres elsewhere. Most of the claims you will encounter are promotional rather than backed by demonstrated inbound demand. That is the honest reading as of 2026.

### Oncology

Oncology makes the limit unusually clear. Serbia's national cancer institute collaborates with foreign proton-therapy centres in Italy to send patients abroad for some advanced treatment. In other words, for certain cutting-edge oncology, Serbs themselves travel out of the country. There is no robust evidence that Belgrade is a net inbound oncology destination. If you are weighing where to go for advanced cancer care, this is exactly the kind of fact that should weigh heavily — and that a promotional site would not mention.

### Bariatric and weight-loss surgery

For bariatric and weight-loss surgery, there is little high-quality evidence of dedicated bariatric tourism into Serbia. European bariatric comparisons rarely feature the country at all, and where it does appear, it tends to be folded into broader plastic-surgery marketing rather than standing as a recognised speciality draw. That is indicative as of 2025, but the absence of evidence is itself the signal here.

## How to read all of this

None of this is a verdict on the quality of individual Serbian doctors or hospitals, and nothing here is medical advice or a recommendation about any procedure. It is a map of where the evidence actually points. Belgrade can be a sensible choice for elective orthopaedics if — and it is a real if — you treat hip or knee replacement as the major surgery it is, plan for weeks of rehabilitation rather than a quick turnaround, and arrange your aftercare before you leave home. The cost side of that calculation lives in our overview of [medical tourism costs in Belgrade](/medical-tourism/medical-tourism-costs-in-belgrade), which is the right companion once you have decided the procedure itself makes sense.

For advanced cardiology, oncology and bariatric care, the honest answer is different. The evidence does not support travelling to Serbia specifically for these, and in oncology the national institution's own behaviour points outward, not in. That candour is the most valuable thing this page can offer: knowing what Belgrade is good for is useful, but knowing what it is not the destination for can matter far more.
