## What is Serbian street food, and what should I eat first?

Serbian street food is built on grilled minced meat and bakery pastry — cheap, fast, meat-heavy and genuinely good. If you only learn three words of Serbian food, make them **[ćevapi](/glossary/cevapi)**, **[pljeskavica](/glossary/pljeskavica)** and **[burek](/glossary/burek)**. Together they cover most of what Belgrade eats on the go, and all three are inexpensive, quick and reliably satisfying. This page is the canonical home for Belgrade street food and grill — where and how to eat it. For the deeper cultural story of these dishes and the wider Serbian table, see our guide to [Serbian food](/food-and-nightlife/serbian-food).

**Ćevapi** (also called ćevapčići) are small grilled minced-meat sausages — beef, pork, lamb or a mix — usually served in or alongside a soft flatbread called lepinja or somun, with chopped raw onion and accompaniments such as kajmak and ajvar. They normally arrive in portions of several pieces (commonly five or ten), so it's a filling order rather than a snack.

**Pljeskavica** is the same minced-meat tradition pressed into a single large grilled patty — the Serbian relative of a burger — and it's often served with kajmak, ajvar and onions too. The **Leskovac style** is a well-known spicy variant, worth asking for if you like heat. A stuffed pljeskavica (filled with cheese or kajmak) is another common upgrade.

**Burek** is a different animal: a flaky filled pastry, made with meat, cheese or other fillings, and it's a staple of both breakfast and the small hours. It's traditionally eaten with **yoghurt**, which cuts through the richness — order the two together and you've got the classic combination.

Two accompaniments tie the grilled dishes together. **Kajmak** is a clotted-cream dairy spread, and **ajvar** is a roasted red-pepper relish — these are the defining sides to grilled meat here, not optional extras. State them by name when you order and you'll eat the way locals do.

## Where can I get the best ćevapi in Belgrade? The venue types

The best ćevapi in Belgrade come from a **ćevabdžinica** or roštilj spot — a counter-service grill — and the most reliable advice is to trust the venue type rather than chase a single name, because the places that serve this food are as durable as the dishes themselves even as individual shops open and close. There are three venue categories you'll use again and again.

The **ćevabdžinica** or roštilj spot is your counter-service or quick-serve grilled-meat place — this is where ćevapi and pljeskavica are at their best, cooked over a grill and handed to you fast. Named spots come and go, but the format is permanent; as of 2026 you'll see well-regarded grill counters both around the centre and in residential neighbourhoods, and the local rule of thumb — follow the smoke and the queue — works better than any list.

The **pekara** (bakery) is found on nearly every corner and serves burek and other pastries around the clock. This is the most casual and the cheapest of the three formats: you point, you pay, you eat standing up or walking. Burek is sold by the slice or piece, the yoghurt is in the fridge next to the till, and many pekare never close at all — which is why they anchor the city's late-night eating (more below). For a visitor, the pekara is also the easiest first transaction in Belgrade: everything is visible behind glass, so no menu decoding is needed.

The **late-night and 24-hour spot** is the third category — grill counters, gyros windows and pizza-by-the-slice places that stay open after the clubs close, alongside the round-the-clock bakeries. They cluster where the nightlife is, and they exist because Belgrade's going-out culture runs to 04:00–06:00 and produces a reliable 3 a.m. appetite.

Note what this page deliberately isn't: the sit-down tavern version of grilled meat. Ćevapi and pljeskavica appear on every [kafana](/glossary/kafana) menu too, as part of a long evening with music — that experience has its own home in our [Belgrade kafanas](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-kafanas) guide. This page is the quick, standing-up, hand-held end of the tradition.

## What's open after the clubs? Belgrade's late-night food

Belgrade has a strong late-night food culture centred on **burek, pizza, gyros and grilled meat**, and the 24-hour pekara is its backbone: a post-club burek with yoghurt is as much a Belgrade institution as the club itself. Among named spots, **Trpković** bakery is repeatedly cited by locals and travellers as a reference point for high-quality burek (reported, as of 2026), and some burek houses advertise founding dates as early as 1908 — treat the exact dates as reported rather than verified, but the longevity itself tells you how durable this corner of the food scene is.

The practical version: wherever you end the night, something will be open within a few blocks. Look for the lit bakery window, or follow the small crowd — the same queue heuristic that finds good ćevapi at lunchtime finds the good burek at 4 a.m.

## What does it cost — and do I need cash?

Grill and street food is the cheapest way to eat in Belgrade, consistently described as very affordable by European-capital standards (indicative, as of 2026) — a bakery breakfast or a grill-counter meal is an everyday expense for locals, not a tourist splurge. Prices move with inflation and the exchange rate, so check the counter's menu board rather than trusting any published figure.

The one mechanical thing to know: **this is the cash-leaning corner of Belgrade eating.** Cards are accepted across most of the city, but bakeries, kiosks and some small grill spots may still be cash-only (indicative, as of 2026), so carry some dinars specifically for street food. Where to change money and how to avoid the bad exchange offices is covered in our [money in Belgrade](/plan-your-trip/money) guide.

## How to order like a local

A short field manual. At a grill counter, name the dish, the size and the sides: ćevapi come in portions (five or ten pieces), pljeskavica by the patty, and you add **kajmak** and **ajvar** by name. At a pekara, point at the burek you want — meat (*sa mesom*) or cheese (*sa sirom*) — and take a drinking yoghurt with it. In touristic areas (Knez Mihailova, Skadarlija, the riverfront) you'll reliably find bilingual menus and some English; in neighbourhood spots the menu may be Serbian-only, and those few food keywords plus pointing will carry you through (reported, as of 2026).

Vegetarians can survive here, but only just: cheese burek and various bakery pastries are the accidental meat-free options, while the grill counters are almost entirely meat. Clarify ingredients if it matters — hidden animal fats and cheese are common, and fillings aren't always labelled.
