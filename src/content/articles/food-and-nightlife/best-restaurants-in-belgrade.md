## Where should you eat in Belgrade?

The short answer: decide what you want the evening to *be*, then pick by area. For a special tasting-menu dinner, head for the fine-dining rooms in and around the old town (Stari Grad). For a river view with your meal, the Sava waterfront at Beton Hala is the obvious play. For traditional Serbian food with live music, go to the cobbled bohemian quarter of Skadarlija. For modern reinterpretations of Balkan cooking, look for the "New Balkan" kitchens scattered across the centre and New Belgrade. The rest of this guide frames the best restaurants in Belgrade exactly that way — by area and by category, with the *why* behind each — rather than as a flat list to tick off.

Two things are worth saying up front because they're stable, and you can rely on them. Belgrade's fine-dining culture has genuinely matured: the Michelin Guide introduced a dedicated Belgrade selection in 2022, a clear signal the city had arrived on the international dining map. And running alongside that recognition is a confident "New Balkan" wave — chefs reinterpreting traditional dishes through seasonal, local sourcing and modern plating. Both of those are durable facts about the city, not trends.

What changes year to year is the detail. The Michelin Belgrade selection grew from around 22 restaurants in the 2024 edition to roughly 25 by the 2026 edition, with the guide now also awarding stars — two starred kitchens in the latest selection (one in the historic centre, one out in the countryside) alongside a small set of Bib Gourmand value picks, spanning contemporary Serbian, Mediterranean, Asian and international cuisines (indicative, as of the 2026 selection). Michelin selections change yearly, so treat any specific count or roster as a snapshot and check the current edition before you book; the venues named throughout this guide are illustrative of their category, not a frozen list. If you want to understand the traditional dishes these kitchens are reworking, our guide to [Serbian food](/food-and-nightlife/serbian-food) explains the building blocks.

## What is fine dining in Belgrade like — and the Michelin picture?

Fine dining in Belgrade clusters in the historic core, where a handful of durable, multiply-recognised rooms form a safe backbone for any trip. These are the restaurants with multi-year recognition, historic settings or long ownership — the ones you can book with confidence.

**Salon 1905** is the city's marquee fine-dining experience, set in the landmark turn-of-the-century Geozavod building near the Sava end of the old town — a masterpiece of mixed Baroque and Art Nouveau architecture, its interior wrapped in brass, marble and gold. It serves a surprise seasonal tasting menu built around top Serbian produce (you choose only the number of courses), has been repeatedly Michelin-listed since the first Belgrade selection, and carries further recognition beyond Michelin (Gault&Millau toques, a La Liste world ranking, JRE membership). The combination of a landmark building and sustained, multi-source recognition makes it a dependable choice (Michelin status as of the 2026 selection).

**Langouste** is a long-running upscale seafood specialist with river views near Kosančićev venac in the historic centre, and in the latest selection it became one of Belgrade's two Michelin-*starred* kitchens, with refined, contemporary interpretations of local products (Michelin-starred as of the 2026 selection — star status is perishable, so confirm before you book). It's a reliable pick when you want fish and a view at the top end.

**Enso** is a contemporary, chef-driven kitchen built around tasting menus and seasonal modern-European cooking, with a multi-year guide presence — durable-leaning. **Klub Književnika** (the Writers' Club) began in 1946 as the Serbian Writers' Association's eatery in the basement of a neoclassical building at Francuska 7 near the centre, opened to the public in the 1950s, and counts a roll-call of literary and international guests in its history; with over 70 years behind it and a current chef-patron kitchen blending Serbian produce with French technique, it's Michelin-listed and the kind of decades-old institution that rewards a visit as much for its atmosphere as its kitchen (longevity confirmed; Michelin status as of the 2026 selection).

Note that the second of Belgrade's two current Michelin stars sits outside the city proper, in the countryside north-west of town, serving French-influenced cooking — a reminder that the region's fine-dining map now reaches beyond the centre (indicative, as of the 2026 selection).

## What are the best traditional Serbian restaurants — and where's the New Balkan scene?

For traditional Serbian cooking, the destination is **Skadarlija**, the cobbled bohemian quarter in Stari Grad just east of Republic Square — a short downhill walk from the square. Its preserved late-19th-century streets are lined with historic kafanas serving grilled meats, shared platters and live old-town (*starogradska*) music; it's the city's showpiece for a traditional dinner. We keep the deep dive on tavern culture and specific kafanas to their own pages, but for orientation this is where to point yourself for the classic experience. **Klub Književnika** (above) doubles as a durable traditional-leaning institution near the centre if you want history with your meal.

If you only take one *modern* idea away, make it the **"New Balkan" movement** — Belgrade's most distinctive and durable culinary theme. These kitchens integrate regional, mountain and village recipes into refined, contemporary menus, often as tasting menus.

**Iva New Balkan Cuisine** is the flagship, holding a Michelin Bib Gourmand for value, with regional ingredients and inventive plating — this is the place to understand what "New Balkan" actually means on a plate (Bib Gourmand as of the 2026 selection). Working in the same spirit are **Bela Reka** — which earned a Bib Gourmand in the latest selection and is known for traditional, generous cooking built on meats and cheeses and close supplier ties, including its own farm (Bib Gourmand as of the 2026 selection) — plus **Na Ćošku** and **Legat 1903**, which rework regional and village recipes into polished menus. As a rule of thumb, restaurants that pair genuinely local ingredients with long ownership tend to be more durable than chef-branded pop-ups or restaurants tied to hotel management contracts. When you're weighing where to spend a special evening, that distinction is a useful filter.

## Where to eat at Beton Hala and for a river view?

For a meal with the Sava in front of you, head to **Beton Hala** — a former industrial warehouse on the Sava waterfront (below the old-town ridge, toward Savamala and the fortress) converted into Belgrade's best-known contemporary dining strip. Cuisines here range across Italian, seafood, steak and fusion, and many venues blur into late-night cocktail bars; the draw is the concentration of upscale rooms with big windows over the water.

The durable anchor here is **Comunale Caffè e Cucina**, one of the most elegant stops on the waterfront — exposed industrial design, high ceilings, huge windows over the Sava, handmade pastas among the best in the city and fresh grilled fish; it's been popular for years and is Michelin-listed (Michelin status as of the 2026 selection). **Ambar** is a long-running rustic-chic option in the same complex, serving traditional Balkan dishes and regional spirits in a trendy room right on the river — illustrative of the area's range. Beyond Beton Hala, **Langouste** (above) near Kosančićev venac offers fish with river views at the fine-dining end, and rooftop hotel lounges supply views from above rather than at water level. Because these riverfront and view tables are in demand, book ahead — especially for a window seat at the weekend.

## Who's in the wider field, and which names are still unproven?

Beyond the anchors, the broader Michelin-listed and well-regarded field is large and varied. It includes **Homa, Magellan, The Square, Ebisu, Metropolitan, SkyLounge, Mezestoran Dvorište, Gušti Mora, JaM**, Pinot and others — a spread of creative bistros, hotel fine dining, seafood houses and New Balkan concepts across the centre, riverfront and New Belgrade. Many of these have operated for years and lean durable. (Examples throughout are illustrative of their category; rosters shift with each Michelin edition.)

A word of honest caution: newer entries such as **The Twenty Two** and **L'Adresse** are worth knowing about, but should be treated as perishable until they prove longevity. They may well become institutions; they aren't yet, so don't book them expecting a settled track record.

## Where do business and investor dinners happen?

For business dinners, the city's corporate and investor dining clusters in hotel restaurants and rooftop lounges — **SkyLounge, Metropolitan Grill** and **The Square** among them — concentrated in New Belgrade (Novi Beograd) and the central hotels, which is more associated with polished lounges and deal-making tables than with traditional kafanas. L'Adresse and similar hotel-adjacent fine-dining rooms fit the same brief. That pattern is indicative as of 2026. A practical durability note: hotel restaurants tied to current management contracts tend to be less durable than owner-run institutions, so for a dinner that matters, the longer-standing anchors are the safer bet.

## Do you need a reservation, and how should you dress?

For the best restaurants in Belgrade, treat a reservation as the default. Fine-dining and Michelin-listed kitchens strongly recommend booking, particularly at weekends and for prime or window seating, and several of the tasting-menu rooms take only a limited number of covers a night; the popular Beton Hala and river-view tables fill up too. Casual bistros and neighbourhood places are usually fine to walk into midweek, but a quick call never hurts for a larger group (indicative, as of 2026).

Dress runs from smart-casual to elegant at the top rooms; you won't need a jacket-and-tie level of formality, but shorts and sportswear feel out of place at fine dining. On price: Belgrade is noticeably cheaper than Western European capitals — cost-of-living trackers put a typical restaurant meal here well below the US and Western-EU average, and even the city's priciest tasting menus cost a fraction of comparable Paris or London tables (indicative, as of 2026). Prices move with inflation and exchange rates, though, so rather than quote figures that will quickly date, check current menus directly before you book.

## What about wine?

Many of these serious kitchens double as wine venues, showcasing Serbian wine regions through their lists — for non-specialists, dinner at a restaurant with a strong list is often the most practical introduction to Serbian wine. We won't go deep on that here, though — if wine is your focus, our dedicated guide to [Belgrade wine bars and rakija](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-wine-bars) covers the dedicated wine venues and the broader scene. ## What's still unknown

Because Michelin revises its selection every year, the precise count of listed restaurants, the star awards and the exact roster will shift; the figures here belong to the 2024–2026 editions and nothing should be read as a permanent number. The newer venues flagged above have not yet proven the kind of longevity that would let us recommend them as settled institutions, and star status in particular is perishable — confirm it before you build a trip around it. What is not in doubt — and what you can plan around with confidence — is that Belgrade has a real, maturing Michelin selection and a genuine New Balkan movement, anchored by the durable restaurants described here.
