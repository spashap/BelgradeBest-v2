## A scene that grew up quickly

Belgrade's fine-dining culture has matured into something genuinely worth planning around. The Michelin Guide introduced a dedicated Belgrade selection in 2022, a clear signal that the city had arrived on the international dining map. Alongside that recognition runs a confident "New Balkan" wave — chefs reinterpreting traditional dishes through seasonal, local sourcing and modern plating. Both of these are stable facts about the city, and you can rely on them.

What changes year to year is the detail. By the 2024 edition, the Michelin Belgrade selection highlighted around 22 restaurants, including Bib Gourmand value picks, spanning contemporary Serbian, Mediterranean, Asian and international cuisines. That figure is indicative as of the 2024 selection — Michelin selections change yearly, so treat any specific count as a snapshot and check the current edition before you book. If you want to understand the traditional dishes these kitchens are reworking, our guide to [Serbian food](/food-and-nightlife/serbian-food) explains the building blocks.

## Durable anchors worth booking

These are the restaurants with multi-year recognition, historic settings or long ownership — the safest recommendations, and a sensible backbone for any trip.

**Salon 1905** offers fine dining in a historic Art Nouveau building, with a Serbian and international menu, and has been repeatedly Michelin-listed since the first Belgrade selection. The combination of a landmark building and sustained recognition makes it a dependable choice.

**Iva New Balkan Cuisine** is the flagship of the modernised-Balkan approach, holding a Michelin Bib Gourmand for value. Expect regional ingredients and inventive plating — this is the place to understand what "New Balkan" actually means on a plate.

**Klub Književnika** began as a post-WW2 literary café and is now a restaurant with over 70 years of history, and is Michelin-listed. It is the kind of decades-old institution that rewards a visit as much for its atmosphere as its kitchen.

**Langouste** is a long-running upscale seafood specialist with river views near Kosančićev venac, and is Michelin-listed — a reliable option when you want fish and a view.

**Comunale Caffè e Cucina**, in Beton Hala on the riverfront, serves shared-plates Italian and has been popular for years; it too is Michelin-listed. Its prime riverside location and longevity make it an easy recommendation.

**Enso** is a contemporary, chef-driven kitchen built around tasting menus and seasonal modern-European cooking, with a multi-year guide presence. **Istok**, meanwhile, is a Vietnamese bistro that earned a Bib Gourmand in the 2024 selection, praised for authentic, generous dishes — a more niche cuisine, but a durable concept.

## The wider listed field

Beyond the anchors, the broader Michelin-listed and well-regarded field is large and varied. It includes Homa, Magellan, The Square, Ebisu, Metropolitan, SkyLounge, Na Ćošku, Mezestoran Dvorište, Bela Reka, Gušti Mora, JaM, Legat 1903 and others — a spread of creative bistros, hotel fine dining, seafood houses and new-Balkan concepts. Many of these have operated for years and lean durable.

A word of honest caution: newer entries such as The Twenty Two and L'Adresse are worth knowing about, but should be treated as perishable until they prove longevity. They may well become institutions; they aren't yet, so don't book them expecting a settled track record.

For business dinners, the city's investor and corporate dining clusters in hotel restaurants and rooftop lounges — SkyLounge, Metropolitan Grill and The Square among them — concentrated in New Belgrade and the central hotels. That pattern is indicative as of 2026.

## New Balkan — the theme that lasts

If you only take one idea away, make it this one. The "New Balkan" movement is Belgrade's most distinctive and durable culinary theme. Iva is its flagship, but places like Legat 1903, Na Ćošku and Bela Reka work in the same spirit — integrating regional and village recipes into refined, modern menus. As a rule of thumb, restaurants that pair genuinely local ingredients with long ownership tend to be more durable than chef-branded pop-ups or restaurants tied to hotel management contracts. When you're weighing where to spend a special evening, that distinction is a useful filter.

## Practicalities and a note on wine

A few sensible habits will serve you well. Fine-dining and Michelin-listed restaurants strongly recommend reservations, particularly at weekends, and dress tends to run from smart-casual to elegant. Prices move, so rather than quote figures that will quickly date, the honest advice is to check current menus directly before you book.

Many of these serious kitchens double as wine venues, showcasing Serbian wine regions through their lists. We won't go deep on that here — if wine is your focus, our dedicated guide to [Belgrade wine bars and rakija](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-wine-bars) covers the dedicated wine venues and the broader scene. Districts are mentioned above only for orientation; for the wider picture of neighbourhoods and what else to do around your dinner reservation, see [things to do in Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/things-to-do-in-belgrade).

## What's still unknown

Because Michelin revises its selection every year, the precise count of listed restaurants and the exact roster will shift; the around-22 figure belongs to the 2024 edition and nothing here should be read as a permanent number. The newer venues flagged above have not yet announced or proven the kind of longevity that would let us recommend them as settled institutions. What is not in doubt — and what you can plan around with confidence — is that Belgrade has a Michelin selection and a real New Balkan movement, anchored by the durable restaurants listed here.
