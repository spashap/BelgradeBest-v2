## What are the splavovi in Belgrade?

The *splavovi* (singular *splav*) are Belgrade's floating river clubs and bars — moored structures resting on the water rather than on dry land — and they are the city's signature nightlife format. The word *splav* simply means a floating structure tied to the riverbank, and in Belgrade it covers a wide spectrum: casual riverside bars, sit-down grill and seafood restaurants, and high-capacity nightclubs, all of them on the Sava and the Danube. They are widely used by locals and visitors alike to escape the summer heat. The rivers have long been described as lined with around 300 splavovi of various types (a figure that is fluid — a 2026 local article put the working number at "more than 200," and the true count changes yearly, especially after the recent clean-up described below). Some run year-round, but many operate only in summer — which brings us to the single most important thing to understand before you plan a night out.

The scene itself is the durable institution here; individual clubs come and go, and right now the whole zone is being reorganised. So this guide is built around how the scene works for a visitor — season, geography, booking, etiquette and price mechanics — rather than a venue list you should treat as fixed.

## When is splav season — and why does timing matter so much?

Plan around **May through September**, with **June** the month when full programming is typically in place. Belgrade nightlife is explicitly seasonal. At the end of spring, the major indoor clubs close or scale back, and the high-energy crowds move to the riverbank splavovi. In winter the same crowds — and often the same operators — move back indoors.

This matters enormously for timing. If you arrive in January expecting a riverside club night, you will find the scene has migrated inside. The floating clubs are a warm-weather institution; a visitor planning around them should aim for late spring through early autumn. If your trip falls outside that window, treat the splavovi as a thing to file away for next time and look instead at the broader range of [things to do in Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/things-to-do-in-belgrade).

## Are the splavovi still open in 2026? (What changed on the quays)

Short answer: **yes, the scene is still running in 2026 — but it is mid-shake-up, so don't assume a specific raft is still where last year's guide put it.** This is the freshest and most important thing to know, and most older English-language guides have not caught up with it.

Over 2024 and 2025 the city ran a multi-year river clean-up and regulation drive. Many of the older splavovi — a lot of them informally or illegally moored — were towed away in phases from their original quays. The stretch along the **Novi Beograd Savski kej** (the Sava promenade by the New Belgrade blocks) and the quay near the **Ušće** confluence were cleared, and the last raft on that stretch was reported removed in October 2025 (REPORTED, as of late 2025). Displaced splavovi were moved variously toward the **Fair / Sajmište** area and to the **Gročanski kej** further downstream, with their long-term positions still unsettled.

The city's stated plan is to replace the roughly 100 informal rafts that once lined the Sava promenade with a much smaller number of **regulated, licensed splavovi** — on the order of 28–30 tendered locations, spaced apart, connected to proper water, electricity and wastewater infrastructure, in standardised sizes. In other words, the river is expected to end up with substantially fewer splavovi than before — by some accounts up to two-thirds fewer — but better-regulated ones (REPORTED, city plan as of 2025–2026; figures and timeline are perishable and have been described as running behind schedule).

What this means for you as a visitor in 2026: the institution is intact and the summer season still happens, but the **map is in flux**. The names, the count and the exact quay where the busiest clubs cluster are all changing. Treat any specific venue or location below as illustrative of a *zone and a type*, and confirm the current cluster locally — ask your accommodation, check a current local listings site, or look at where the lights and crowds actually are — rather than trusting a fixed list.

## Where are the splavovi — Sava vs Danube?

Historically, most splavovi clustered in a few waterfront stretches, and it helps to orient by river:

- **The Sava side** is where the largest clubbing rafts have concentrated. The **Savski kej** — the Sava promenade along the New Belgrade blocks — and the pier behind the **Belgrade Fair** (Sajamski kej / Sajmište), on the Sava between the Gazela and the railway bridges, were the historic heart of the loud, high-capacity club scene. **Savamala**, on the right bank of the Sava just below the old town ridge between the Branko bridge and the old Sava port, is the riverside district most associated with the biggest river clubs; adult-nightlife guides describe venues there with capacity for over 2,000 people, with parties reported to peak between roughly 02:00 and 04:00 (REPORTED).
- **The Danube side**, including the **Ušće** quay at the confluence of the Sava and the Danube, has hosted a mix of clubbing and more relaxed venues, and is home to some of the longest-running names. Restaurant-style and grill/seafood splavovi — calmer, more family-friendly, and more durable — cluster further out, including along the Danube around **Zemun**.

A rough rule of thumb that has held: **the Sava stretches lean louder, bigger and more club-driven; the Danube and the Zemun riverfront lean calmer, with more restaurant-style and relaxed floating venues.** Treat this as a tendency, not a hard line — and remember the clean-up above is currently reshuffling exactly which quay hosts what.

Savamala has its own distinct character as a district; if you want to understand the area beyond its nightlife role, it is worth reading about Belgrade's [neighbourhoods](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-neighborhoods) separately. For a night out, what you need to know is simply that the river clubs concentrate along these waterfront stretches rather than being scattered across the city — and that the current concentration is in motion.

## What kind of music and venues will you find?

Splavovi split broadly by music. Some are R&B, hip-hop and house-oriented dance clubs; others lean electronic and techno; and turbo-folk venues are part of the mix too. Programming changes from venue to venue and from season to season, so it is best to think in categories rather than fixed line-ups.

A note on the electronic/techno end: some of the most-cited underground-electronic spots (for example the small boat **20/44**, and warehouse-style venues such as Drugstore) sit on or near the river but are not classic high-capacity splav format — they're smaller boats and riverside warehouses profiled in techno-scene guides. So "river clubs" in Belgrade spans both the big mainstream party rafts and a more underground boat/warehouse scene nearby.

One name stands out for sheer longevity. **Freestyler** is a long-running Danube splav, widely cited as operating since 2001 and specialising in R&B, hip-hop and house — more than twenty years of operation and heavy international coverage make it a durable example (it has typically run roughly Thursday–Sunday late-night in summer, though specific nights and hours are perishable). Other frequently-named clubbing splavovi over the years have included venues such as Leto, Money Club, Lasta and Tag along the Sava stretch. Treat **all** of these as illustrations of types and zones rather than guarantees: the scene itself is the institution, while any single club's current existence, location and operating status is something to verify when you arrive — doubly so given the 2024–2025 reshuffle.

## Do you need a reservation, and what's the dress code?

**Reservation culture is strong**, and for the large-capacity splavovi and the more exclusive clubs, reservations are effectively required — especially at weekends. Nightlife guides describe them as "must have" for venues holding several hundred people or more. Turning up without one at a busy venue can mean being turned away or left without a place to sit. Casual riverside bars and restaurant-style splavovi are far more walk-in friendly.

**Dress code is typically smart-casual at minimum.** Many venues ban sandals for men and enforce an "elegant or smart-casual" door policy; reviewers note this is sometimes enforced without being posted anywhere, so don't rely on seeing a sign. Avoid sportswear, shorts and flip-flops at the mainstream clubs. Specifics vary by venue and change over time.

**On ID:** Serbia's legal drinking age is 18. ID checks are more common at busy nightclubs than at sit-down venues, and younger-looking guests are more likely to be carded, so it is sensible to carry identification.

## How does splav pricing actually work?

This is where visitors most often get caught out. **Entry is frequently advertised as free — but a seated table carries a minimum spend**, so "free entry" does not mean a cheap night at a prime table.

As an indicative example from 2024, one major splav's published list set beer from around 590 RSD, cocktails from around 890 RSD, and table bottle minimums from around 16,900 RSD (indicative, as of 2024; check current menus). A Savamala adult-nightlife guide gives a similar shape: entry free to around 1,500 RSD, beers around 300–500 RSD, cocktails around 700–1,200 RSD, and bottle service starting around 6,000–15,000 RSD (indicative, prices collected around 2025–2026).

For a rough benchmark, aggregated 2025 club and splav drink prices put beer at roughly 2–4 EUR, cocktails at roughly 5–7 EUR, a domestic wine bottle around 25–40 EUR, mid-range vodka around 80–100 EUR, and champagne anywhere from around 90 to 270 EUR (indicative, as of 2025; fluctuates with inflation and the exchange rate). Most mid-range and high-end splavovi take cards.

The practical takeaway: the cost of a night depends far less on the entry line than on whether you take a table and what its minimum is. Those figures are illustrative and change, so always check current menus.

## How do you avoid the tourist traps?

Mainstream splavovi are generally described as safe if you respect the dress code, book ahead, and follow local norms. The recurring problems are commercial rather than dangerous:

- **Bottle-service tables.** Watch for overpriced or effectively mandatory bottle-service tables aimed at groups without local contacts.
- **Per-table vs per-person minimums.** Be clear on whether a minimum spend is per table or per person — confirm this before you sit down, because the difference is large.
- **Ad-hoc door fees.** On high-demand nights, some nominally "free entry" clubs apply ad-hoc entry fees at the door.

Booking directly, or through a reputable agency, reduces all of these risks.

Getting to and between the river clubs is its own subject; for late-night movement around the city, including how to choose a reputable transport option, see the guidance on [getting around Belgrade](/plan-your-trip/getting-around). When you do head home late from the waterfront, stick to a reputable transport option rather than whatever is idling outside the gate.

## What to keep in mind

Specific clubs come and go — and right now the quays themselves are being reorganised — but the format is stable. Plan around the **May–September** season, **book ahead** for the big venues, **dress smart-casual**, **carry ID**, **clarify any table minimum before you sit**, and **confirm the current cluster locally** rather than trusting last year's venue map. Do that, and the splavovi deliver exactly what they are famous for.
