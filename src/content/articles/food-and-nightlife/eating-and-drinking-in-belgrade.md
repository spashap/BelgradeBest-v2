## What is Belgrade's food and drink scene like?

Belgrade is an easy, late, good-value city to eat and drink in, and its scene is organised around a handful of recognisable formats rather than one dining style. The core of it is the **kafana** — a traditional tavern that doubles as restaurant, café, pub and live-music venue — alongside modern and fine-dining **restaurants**, riverside floating clubs called **splavovi**, a dense **café and coffee** culture, **wine bars** and **craft-beer** taprooms, **pastry shops** (poslastičarnice), street-food **grills and bakeries**, and a small but growing **vegetarian and vegan** offer.

The scene is strongly **seasonal**. In winter, nightlife concentrates in indoor clubs, bars and warm kafanas; from late spring through summer — roughly May to September — the high-energy nightlife moves to the splavovi moored on the Sava and Danube, and riverfront dining takes over. Café, coffee and pastry culture runs year-round.

## What food is Belgrade known for?

Belgrade is a grilled-meat city first. The signatures are **ćevapi** (small grilled minced-meat fingers) and **pljeskavica** (a large Serbian-style burger patty), eaten with raw onion, **kajmak** (a rich cream-cheese spread) and **lepinja** flatbread. The other thing everyone eats is **burek** — a flaky, filled pastry (meat, cheese or spinach) that is the classic breakfast and the standard food after a night out.

Beyond the grill, the dishes that define a proper sit-down Serbian meal include **karađorđeva šnicla** (a rolled, breaded cutlet stuffed with kajmak — Serbia's answer to cordon bleu), **sarma** (cabbage rolls stuffed with minced meat and rice, slow-cooked for hours), and shared **meze** starters. The national drink is **rakija**, a strong fruit brandy — plum **šljivovica** above all — traditionally taken as a small pour before the meal with a toast of "Živeli!".

The scene is broader than the classics, though: Belgrade now has a maturing fine-dining and "New Balkan" restaurant culture (the Michelin Guide added a dedicated Belgrade selection in 2022 and listed 22 restaurants in its 2024 edition), a serious craft-beer scene, a wine renaissance built on indigenous Serbian grapes, and a deep pastry-shop tradition. For the dishes in full, see [Serbian food](/food-and-nightlife/serbian-food) and [grill and street food](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-grill-and-street-food).

## What should I eat and drink in Belgrade?

If you only have a few meals, a good arc is: a **grill plate** (ćevapi or pljeskavica with kajmak and onion) for a casual lunch; a **burek** for breakfast or a post-club snack; and one proper **kafana dinner** with shared starters, grilled meat, sides and live tamburica music — that is where you try karađorđeva šnicla, sarma, prebranac (a baked bean dish that is often accidentally vegan) and a round of rakija.

To drink: **rakija** is the cultural must-try, but Belgrade also rewards a stop at a **craft-beer** taproom and a **wine bar** pouring Serbian labels from Fruška Gora, Šumadija and other regions. Daytime, lean into the **café and coffee** culture — sitting over a single coffee for an hour is the local way. Finish at a **poslastičarnica** (pastry shop) for an Ottoman- or Austro-Hungarian-influenced sweet such as a Moskva šnit cake, or modern gelato. Each of these has a dedicated guide — [kafanas](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-kafanas), [craft beer](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-craft-beer), [wine bars](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-wine-bars) and [coffee culture](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-coffee-culture) — so use this page to plan the shape of your eating and route to the detail.

## How much does food and drink cost in Belgrade?

Belgrade is inexpensive by Western-European standards, which is one of its real pleasures (indicative, as of 2026). As reported benchmarks: a meal at a casual restaurant runs roughly €10 per person, and a full sit-down dinner with a drink, coffee and dessert is commonly around €12–20 per person. A pljeskavica from a grill is reported at around 450 RSD, and a fast-food combo around 1,000 RSD.

For drinks: a coffee is commonly reported at around 150–300 RSD in non-touristy cafés (indicative, as of 2026), and higher in prime spots; a cappuccino sits around 250–260 RSD and a draft local beer around 300–350 RSD (reported, as of 2026). On the river, club and splav drinks run higher — roughly 2–4 EUR for a beer and roughly 5–7 EUR for a cocktail (reported, as of 2026).

A couple of concrete examples worth budgeting against, all reported and time-stamped:

- A 2024 splav table pricelist listed beer from about 590 RSD, cocktails from about 890 RSD, and bottle-table minimums from around 16,900 RSD — entry was advertised as free, but tables carried a minimum spend.
- A full kafana dinner for two at a mid-range central or Skadarlija venue was reported at around 4,000–8,000 RSD (indicative, as of 2026).

Treat every figure here as a reported benchmark rather than a fixed price: prices shift with inflation and the exchange rate, so check current menus before you go. The mechanics below — how you actually pay, tip and book — are the durable part.

## Paying: cards work, but keep cash for the small places

Cards are widely accepted across urban Belgrade. Most mid-range and high-end restaurants, the river barges known as splavovi, and hotel bars will take a card without fuss. Where cash still matters is at the smaller end of the scale: kafanas (especially for tips), bakeries, markets, dessert shops, kiosks and some older local bars.

Travellers commonly report being able to settle roughly 70–90% of their city expenses by card (indicative, as of 2026), but the sensible habit is to always carry some dinars for the cash-leaning spots. As a rule of thumb, assume card at restaurants, splavovi and hotel bars; assume cash for everything small and local. For the wider question of exchanging and handling money, see [money in Belgrade](/plan-your-trip/money) — this page sticks to how you pay inside venues.

## Tipping: discretionary, and usually in cash

There is no legally enforced tipping norm in Serbia. Tipping is discretionary appreciation for good service, not an obligation. The local consensus, reported as the Belgrade custom (indicative, as of 2026), is to round up small café and restaurant bills and to leave around 5–10% on full meals or for larger groups when the service has been good.

One practical detail worth knowing: even when you pay the bill by card, tipping is usually done in cash. This is another reason to keep a few notes on you. Hand the tip directly to your server rather than expecting to add it to the card total. In a kafana, the live musicians who circulate between tables are tipped separately and directly, usually in cash — see [kafanas](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-kafanas) for how that works.

## Hours and the going-out rhythm

Belgrade runs late. Many restaurants serve until around 23:00 or later, while bars and clubs commonly stay open until roughly 04:00–06:00. This is a city where dinner can start at nine and the evening keeps going from there.

The big going-out nights are typically described as Thursday through Sunday, with some venues running until about 08:00 (reported, as of 2026). Waterfront venues in particular blend dining and nightlife with long daily hours — some Beton Hala spots list hours from 10:00 to 04:00 daily — so the line between "restaurant" and "club" is often blurred along the river. For the going-out scene in depth, see [Belgrade nightlife](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-nightlife) and, in summer, [splavovi](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-splavovi).

## Reservations and dress codes

Reservations are effectively required for large-capacity splavovi and exclusive clubs, especially at weekends. Fine-dining and Michelin-listed restaurants also strongly recommend booking if you want prime seating. If a place is well known and it's a Friday or Saturday, assume you need to book ahead.

On dress: fine dining is smart-casual to elegant, with shorts and sportswear frowned upon (reported). Splavovi and clubs expect smart-casual as a minimum, and many ban sandals for men and enforce an "elegant or smart-casual" door policy (reported); some have an age limit (23+) on top of the dress code. Specific venue policies change, so when in doubt, dress up rather than down.

## Drinking age and ID

Serbia's legal drinking age is 18, and the law targets sales to minors. ID checks are more common at busy nightclubs than at sit-down restaurants, and younger-looking guests are more likely to be carded. Carry photo ID if you plan a night out.

## What's open after the clubs close

Belgrade has a strong late-night food culture built around burek, pizza, gyros and grilled meat. The classic post-club move is a 24-hour bakery serving burek — a standard option once the music stops. This page is just the practical "what's open late" note; for the full guide to the grill and bakery scene, see [Belgrade grill and street food](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-grill-and-street-food).

## Where the food scenes cluster by neighbourhood

Belgrade's eating and drinking has clear geography, and knowing it saves a lot of wandering:

- **Skadarlija** (in Stari Grad) is the historic bohemian quarter — cobbled streets of kafanas with live tamburica music and traditional dinners.
- **Dorćol** is the central neighbourhood for specialty coffee, wine bars, craft-beer pubs and modern bistros.
- **Savamala** and the **Sava riverfront** are the heart of the splav and electronic-music scene.
- **Beton Hala**, a converted warehouse strip on the Sava waterfront, packs restaurants and cocktail bars with river views.
- **Zemun**, a historic town now part of Belgrade, is known for riverside fish restaurants and relaxed kafana dining with Danube views.
- **Vračar**, around the Temple of Saint Sava, is café-dense and residential, with brunch spots and dessert shops.
- **New Belgrade (Novi Beograd)** leans towards polished hotel restaurants and business dining.

For the full district-by-district breakdown, see [food and drink by neighbourhood](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-food-and-drink-by-neighborhood).

## Practicalities for foreign visitors

English menus and staff are reliable in the touristic areas — Knez Mihailova, Skadarlija, Beton Hala and the riverfront (reported). Step into a neighbourhood venue and you may find Serbian-only menus with limited English, where a few food keywords or a translation app go a long way (reported).

**Smoking:** indoor smoking is still permitted in many kafanas despite evolving rules, which can surprise visitors from smoke-free countries — worth knowing before you choose a table.

**Dietary needs:** vegetarians can now eat well across mid-range and fine-dining venues, and many places mark vegetarian dishes, but hidden cheese and animal fats are common and dedicated vegan restaurants are few and clustered in the centre — see [vegetarian and vegan Belgrade](/food-and-nightlife/vegetarian-and-vegan-belgrade). Halal Serbian food is hard to find (most halal options are Middle Eastern or fast-food), kosher dining is more limited still, and allergy handling is ad hoc — visitors with serious allergies should talk to staff directly and avoid small kafanas where cross-contamination is likely.

**Tourist-trap warnings** are easy to navigate once you ask the right question up front. The recurring ones: surprise music charges or higher prices on live-music nights at showpiece kafanas, and confusion over whether a splav minimum spend is per-table or per-person — confirm this before you sit down.

For getting between venues, especially late at night, use a reputable transport option; the specifics of [getting around Belgrade](/plan-your-trip/getting-around) live with the transport guide, not here.
