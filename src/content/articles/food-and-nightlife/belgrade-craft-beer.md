## Where the scene sits

Belgrade is widely described as Serbia's craft-beer capital, and for a visitor the practical takeaway is that you don't have to hunt far. The microbreweries, taprooms and specialist beer pubs cluster mainly around Dorćol, Savamala and the central neighbourhoods, which makes a walking circuit feasible in a single evening. Dorćol in particular has become the shorthand for the craft scene, though the cluster spills across the centre. If you want to understand how these districts fit together before you set out, the [Belgrade neighbourhoods](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-neighborhoods) guide covers their character in more detail; this page stays on the beer.

It helps to treat craft beer as its own going-out mode rather than a sub-genre of the club scene. Taprooms and beer gardens are a calmer, daytime-friendly counterpart to the splav (river-raft) nightlife, and the field is mature enough to support guided brewery tours. If you'd rather pair a brewery visit with the wider after-dark picture, the [Belgrade nightlife](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-nightlife) page covers bars and clubs that sit outside this article's scope.

## The flagship breweries

The most durable anchor is **Dogma Brewery**, reported as founded in 2016 in a renovated sugar-factory building near the Sava and Ada Ciganlija. Its on-site taproom and beer garden are reported to have opened in 2017. Dogma is widely cited and has several years of operation behind it, which is part of why brewery tours of the scene tend to include it. The setting — an old industrial building repurposed for brewing — is itself part of the appeal, and the riverside location near Ada Ciganlija makes it a natural daytime stop.

The other long-standing name worth knowing is **Samo Pivo**, a central beer bar that is frequently recommended. It is a durable-leaning fixture rather than a passing arrival, so it's a reasonable place to start if you want somewhere that has held its reputation over time. Between Dogma and Samo Pivo you have two reliable poles for an evening — one a brewery-with-garden, the other a central pub.

## The wider field

Beyond the two anchors, a number of further players come up repeatedly. Frequently-cited names include Docker Brewery, Gunners Pub, Krafter, Draft Bar, Gvint Brewery & Taproom, Brotherhood Beer House and Pivski Zabavnik. Several of these have multi-year histories, but they sit in a volatile bar segment, so it's honest to treat them as examples rather than institutions. Names change, venues move, and some close — so the sensible approach is to confirm a given place is still trading before you build an evening around it.

That caution isn't meant to discourage you. The point of a craft crawl is partly discovery, and the density of the central cluster means that if one taproom has gone, another is usually within walking distance. Use the list above as a starting set of search terms rather than a fixed itinerary, and check current opening hours close to your visit.

## Domestic labels to look for

It's worth understanding the difference between Serbia's mainstream beers and its craft labels. The popular mainstream domestic beers are Jelen and Lav — these are what you'll most often see on standard bar taps and in shops, and they are lagers rather than craft products. On the craft side, domestic labels such as Kabinet and Dogma are commonly recommended; this is indicative as of 2026, so expect the range to evolve. Ordering a Kabinet or a Dogma is a straightforward way to taste what the domestic craft sector is doing without needing to track down a specific taproom.

Knowing these names also helps you read a menu quickly: if a list leans heavily on Jelen and Lav, you're in a mainstream bar; if Kabinet, Dogma and a rotating cast of taproom names appear, you've found the craft end of the spectrum.

## Pricing and what to expect

Pricing is the most perishable part of any beer guide, so treat figures as context rather than a quote. At clubs and splavovi, beer has been reported roughly 2–4 EUR, though that is indicative as of 2025–2026 and you should check current menus. Taproom pricing varies and a craft pour will often sit at the upper end of, or above, that range — which is normal for small-batch beer anywhere. The honest advice is to read the menu rather than rely on a remembered number.

In practice, an evening on the craft scene looks less like club-going and more like a relaxed wander: a taproom or beer garden, a couple of small pours, and a short walk to the next place. The concentration in the Dorćol and central cluster is what makes this work without much planning. If you're slotting a brewery visit into a wider itinerary, the [things to do in Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/things-to-do-in-belgrade) guide can help you balance it against the rest of the city.

## What's still unknown

The durable facts here are clear: the craft-beer scene exists, it clusters around Dorćol and the centre, and Dogma and Samo Pivo are its more established anchors. Most of the rest — the wider list of taprooms and pubs, and the prices — sits in a volatile, perishable segment. The specific named venues in the wider field should be verified at the time of your visit, and any price you see quoted should be checked against a current menu. Where this article can't confirm something as settled, it's better to arrive curious and check on the ground than to treat a list as fixed.
