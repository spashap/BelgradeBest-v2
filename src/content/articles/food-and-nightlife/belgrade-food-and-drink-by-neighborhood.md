## Where to eat and drink in Belgrade, by neighbourhood — how to read this map

Where to eat and drink in Belgrade is really a question of *which district*. The city's food and nightlife cluster by area, and each area has its own mood rather than its own menu. One district is built around long traditional dinners with music; another around quiet flat whites and natural wine; a third around late nights on the river; a fourth around cocktails on a converted waterfront. Knowing which is which lets you pick an evening to suit your appetite instead of chasing single venues across town.

Below is each district's food-and-drink personality in turn, with a quick note on where it sits so you can orient yourself. This page is deliberately **geography-first**: it maps the *character* of each neighbourhood's eating and drinking, not the named venues. For who owns which named places — the actual [kafanas](/glossary/kafana), clubs, coffee houses, wine bars and restaurants — follow the format guides we cross-link as we go: [Belgrade kafanas](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-kafanas), [Belgrade nightlife and splavovi](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-nightlife), and the [best restaurants in Belgrade](/food-and-nightlife/best-restaurants-in-belgrade). For what these neighbourhoods actually *are* — their history, layout and sights — see the wider guide to [Belgrade's neighbourhoods](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-neighborhoods); here we stay strictly on the table and the glass.

## Skadarlija — kafana country

**Where it sits:** [Skadarlija](/glossary/skadarlija) is in the old town of Stari Grad, about a 5–8 minute downhill walk east of Republic Square and just north-east of Terazije, on and around the cobbled Skadarska Street. It borders Upper Dorćol and the Cetinjska area.

Skadarlija is the most single-minded district on this map. Its food-and-drink identity is almost entirely **kafanas**: long, traditional dinners with live tamburica bands, *starogradska* (old-town) songs and [rakija](/glossary/rakija) on the table, set in a preserved late-19th/early-20th-century bohemian quarter of cobblestones and courtyards. Visitors come for the kafana experience itself rather than for bars or clubs, and coffee or dessert tend to be an afterthought to the meal.

A few things to set expectations. The historic Skadarlija kafanas are heavily touristed, with multilingual menus and live music nightly, and they are priced above neighbourhood averages — treat an evening here as a special experience rather than everyday dining. Some visitors are surprised by music charges or higher prices on live-music nights, so it's worth checking. A full kafana meal for two (shared starters, grilled meat, sides, dessert and drinks) at a mid-range venue here is reported around 4,000–8,000 RSD (indicative, triangulated from menus and diner reports as of 2024–2026).

## Dorćol — the coffee-and-craft cluster

**Where it sits:** immediately north and north-east of Republic Square, sloping toward the Danube — a 10–15 minute walk from the square for the upper part, longer for the riverward lower streets.

Dorćol is where Belgrade's contemporary café culture concentrates: small specialty coffee shops, wine bars and **craft-beer bars** — this is the heart of the city's craft cluster — alongside modern bistros and street-food spots. A coffee in a non-touristy café commonly runs around 150–300 RSD (reported, as of 2026), and the district's specialty cafés routinely double as informal workspaces. The rhythm is daytime-into-evening rather than late-night: slow mornings, unhurried dinners, a taproom or a glass of Serbian wine rather than a dance floor. The named rooms live in our [coffee culture](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-coffee-culture) and [craft beer](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-craft-beer) guides.

## Savamala — nightlife first, food second

**Where it sits:** on the right bank of the Sava, about 0.8–1.0 km south-west of Republic Square, downhill between Branko's bridge and the old Sava port.

Savamala's food-and-drink identity is **nightlife-led**. The former warehouse district is the heartland of the [splavovi](/glossary/splav) and the electronic-music scene, with large river clubs — some reported to hold over 2,000 people — and parties that peak between roughly 02:00 and 04:00 (as of 2026). Food here is secondary: casual grills, street food and a few riverfront restaurants that feed the going-out crowd rather than anchor an evening — eat properly somewhere else, then come here for the night. The door rules, reservations and season live in the [splavovi deep dive](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-splavovi).

## Beton Hala — the polished waterfront

**Where it sits:** the converted warehouse strip on the Sava quay directly below the Kalemegdan ridge, a short downhill walk from Knez Mihailova.

Beton Hala is the dressed-up end of riverside eating: a row of Serbian and international restaurants — Italian, seafood, steak, fusion — plus cocktail bars, all with river views toward New Belgrade. Its defining habit is blending dinner into late-night: some venues run roughly 10:00–04:00 daily (reported, as of 2026), so a table booked for dinner can carry you straight through to the small hours without moving. The crowd is dressier and slightly older than in the bar districts — if Skadarlija is the traditional special-occasion evening, this is the contemporary one.

## Zemun — fish restaurants on the Danube

**Where it sits:** north-west of the centre on the Danube bank, beyond New Belgrade — a formerly separate town that still feels like one.

Zemun's food identity leans to the river: **fish restaurants**, river specialities and relaxed kafana-style dining along the Danube quay, plus its own green market feeding a genuinely local food scene. Nightlife is much calmer than on the central splavovi — the classic Zemun evening is a long fish dinner with a water view, not a club night — and the whole pace is closer to a small town's than to central Belgrade's. For what Zemun *is* — the Habsburg history, Gardoš hill, the old streets — see our [Zemun guide](/visit-belgrade/zemun); come back here for the dinner plan.

## Vračar — the everyday neighbourhood table

**Where it sits:** south-east of the old-town core, the residential district around the Temple of Saint Sava.

Vračar is the district for eating the way residents eat: neighbourhood restaurants, brunch spots and dessert shops clustered around the Temple of Saint Sava, with a dense café scene whose specialty rooms double as informal workspaces. Nothing here is a headline destination, and that is the point — this is where you go for a relaxed brunch, a casual dinner or a pastry stop between sights, at everyday prices rather than occasion ones.

## New Belgrade — hotel and business dining

**Where it sits:** across the Sava from the old town — the planned modern city of blocks and boulevards.

New Belgrade's food-and-drink identity is **hotel and business dining**: restaurants and rooftop lounges inside the big business hotels, with international menus and river or skyline views, plus mall-adjacent venues. It is the natural district for a working dinner or a polished cocktail with a view, and the least natural one for kafana culture — the traditional scene lives across the river.

## How to choose your evening

Match the district to the night you want: Skadarlija for the traditional music-and-rakija dinner, Dorćol for coffee, craft beer and an easy evening, Savamala for the club night, Beton Hala for the dressed-up waterfront dinner, Zemun for slow fish by the Danube, Vračar for everyday eating, New Belgrade for business. The paying, tipping and booking mechanics are the same everywhere — they live on [how eating and drinking in Belgrade actually works](/food-and-nightlife/eating-and-drinking-in-belgrade).
