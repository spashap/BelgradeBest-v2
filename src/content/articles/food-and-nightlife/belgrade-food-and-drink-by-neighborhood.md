## How to read this map

Think of Belgrade's eating and drinking as a series of moods rather than a single scene. One district is built around long traditional dinners; another around quiet flat whites and natural wine; a third around late nights on the river. Knowing which is which lets you pick an evening to suit your appetite. Below is each district's food-and-drink personality in turn. For what these neighbourhoods actually *are* — their history, layout and sights — see the wider guide to [Belgrade's neighbourhoods](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-neighborhoods); here we stay strictly on the table and the glass.

## Skadarlija — kafana country

Skadarlija, in the old town of Stari Grad, is the most single-minded district on this map. Its food-and-drink identity is almost entirely kafanas: long, traditional dinners with live tamburica music and rakija on the table. Visitors come for the kafana experience itself rather than for bars or clubs, and coffee or dessert tend to be an afterthought to the meal. If you want one evening that feels unmistakably old-Belgrade, this is where it happens. For the full picture of what a kafana is and how to approach an evening there, see the guide to [Belgrade kafanas](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-kafanas).

## Dorćol — coffee and craft

Dorćol is the city's coffee-and-craft cluster. Its character centres on small cafés and specialty coffee, wine bars and craft-beer bars, alongside modern bistros and street-food spots. This is the district for a slow morning over a carefully made coffee, an afternoon tasting your way through local wines, or an unhurried bistro dinner — a more contemporary, lower-key rhythm than the traditional dinners a few streets away. It rewards wandering rather than booking, and suits visitors who like to drink well without committing to a big night out.

## Savamala and the riverfront — nightlife first

Savamala's personality is nightlife-led. This is the splav and electronic-music heartland, home to the large river clubs that give Belgrade much of its going-out reputation. Food here is firmly secondary to the scene: expect casual grills, street food and some riverfront restaurants rather than destination dining. Come to Savamala for the night itself — the music and the floating clubs — and treat eating as fuel for it rather than the main event.

## Beton Hala — the polished waterfront

A short distance away on the Sava waterfront, Beton Hala offers a more refined register. Set in a converted warehouse, it is a polished dining-and-drinks strip of Serbian and international restaurants with river views, cocktail bars, and venues designed to blend dinner into a late night. Where Savamala is loose and club-driven, Beton Hala is dressier and more dinner-led — a good choice if you want river-view eating and cocktails in one place, easing from the table into the evening without changing district.

## Zemun — the river table

Zemun has its own, gentler character. Its food-and-drink identity leans to riverside fish restaurants, river specialities and relaxed, kafana-style dining along the Danube. The nightlife here is calmer than the central splavovi — this is a place for long dinners rather than clubs. Zemun also has its own green market, which fits its slower, neighbourhood feel. Come here when you want river fish and an unhurried evening rather than volume and crowds. Zemun is also a destination in its own right, with plenty to see beyond the waterfront tables; for that side of it, see the dedicated guide to [Zemun](/visit-belgrade/zemun).

## Vračar — the neighbourhood table

Vračar is residential and café-dense. Its food-and-drink life is built around neighbourhood restaurants, brunch spots and dessert shops, clustered in the streets around the Temple of Saint Sava. This is the district for everyday eating done well — a relaxed brunch, a casual local dinner, coffee and cake in the afternoon — rather than spectacle. It suits visitors who want to eat the way residents do, away from the riverfront and the tourist-facing strips.

## New Belgrade — hotels and skyline

Across the river, New Belgrade (Novi Beograd) reads quite differently again. Its food-and-drink identity is hotel and business dining: large business-hotel restaurants and rooftop lounges with international menus and river or skyline views, rather than traditional kafana culture. If your evening calls for a polished international meal, a rooftop drink, or somewhere that works for business as much as pleasure, this is the district that delivers it — just don't come expecting old-town atmosphere.

## Choosing your evening

Lined up together, the districts sort themselves by mood. For traditional dinners with music, Skadarlija. For coffee, wine and craft beer at a gentle pace, Dorćol. For late nights on the river, Savamala; for polished waterfront dining that drifts into the small hours, Beton Hala. For river fish and a calmer evening, Zemun. For everyday neighbourhood eating, Vračar. And for international menus and skyline views, New Belgrade.

This page is the by-district map; the specifics live elsewhere. Named venues, prices and opening hours change, so this guide deliberately describes each district's durable character rather than cataloguing addresses. When you've settled on a district, follow the relevant format guide — kafanas, river clubs, coffee, wine, craft beer or restaurants — for the named places worth your time. Those details are perishable by nature; the personality of each district, by contrast, has stayed remarkably stable.
