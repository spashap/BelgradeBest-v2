## What a kafana actually is

The simplest way to understand a kafana is to stop trying to map it onto a single English word. A kafana is more than a tavern: it is an all-day social space that combines the roles of restaurant, café, pub and music venue. Its roots run back to the Ottoman coffeehouse, and over time it evolved into a place for eating, drinking and live music all at once. You can sit down for a morning coffee, a long lunch, or a late night of grilled meat and singing, and it is still the same institution.

The look is consistent enough that you will recognise it quickly. Kafanas are characterised by simple wooden interiors, traditional hearty food, long social stays, and often live *starogradska* — old-town — music played by tamburica or accordion bands. Nobody is rushing you out. The point is to linger.

The food itself belongs to a broader story than this page can tell. For the dishes you will meet — the grilled meats, the spreads, the stews — read our guide to [Serbian food](/food-and-nightlife/serbian-food), which is the proper home for the cuisine. Here we are explaining the room, the rhythm and the etiquette rather than the menu.

## Skadarlija and the historic kafanas

If there is one place a visitor is sent first, it is Skadarlija. This is Belgrade's emblematic bohemian quarter: a cobbled street lined with kafanas offering live tamburica music, traditional dishes and late-night socialising. It is genuinely atmospheric, and it is also the most concentrated, most visitor-facing version of kafana culture in the city.

The oldest name in the city's kafana lore sits a little away from that street. The **"?" (Znak Pitanja)** kafana is widely cited as the oldest in Belgrade and is reported to date to the 19th century, originally an Ottoman-era coffeehouse and now serving traditional dishes in a preserved historic interior. Treat the precise founding detail as reported rather than settled, but the institution itself is durable and safe to seek out.

Skadarlija's showpiece kafanas — **Tri Šešira, Dva Jelena, Mali Vrabac, Velika Skadarlija** and others — are long-established institutions with nightly live music and multilingual menus. They are reported to be tourist-facing and priced above neighbourhood averages, which is exactly why they are best framed as "showpiece" experiences: authentic in food and music, but staged for visitors and occasions. The names are durable; any prices or hours you find are perishable, so check current menus.

Skadarlija sits within a wider city, and the character of the surrounding districts is worth understanding on its own terms — for that, see our overview of [Belgrade's neighbourhoods](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-neighborhoods). Here we describe only Skadarlija's role as a kafana street.

## The etiquette and the experience

The single most useful thing to know is that a kafana is a place to linger. Meals last hours, with rounds of food, drink and music rather than a quick plate and a bill. Sharing platters from the centre of the table is common rather than individual plating, so order to the middle and reach in — that is the expected behaviour, not a faux pas.

Music is part of the experience and part of the etiquette. Live bands are reported to circulate between tables taking song requests, and guests are expected to tip the musicians directly, usually in cash. Dancing late in the night is reported as not unusual. Rakija is central to the evening: meals are often started or punctuated with rounds of fruit brandy and toasts, so do not be surprised if a small glass arrives before you have decided on anything else.

One practical warning for visitors from smoke-free countries: smoking indoors is reported to still be permitted in many kafanas despite evolving regulations, and this can be a genuine surprise. This is an indicative posture as of 2026, and it varies from place to place.

## Showpiece versus neighbourhood kafanas

A useful mental split for a first-time visitor is between the showpiece kafanas and the neighbourhood ones. Skadarlija's kafanas are reported to be authentic in their food and music but geared towards visitors and occasions. The neighbourhood kafanas — found across Dorćol, Vračar and outlying districts — are reported to be more local in orientation: simpler, cheaper, with less English on offer, and a deeper everyday culture.

Neither is more "real" than the other; they serve different moods. A showpiece kafana is the easier introduction, with English-capable staff and a band already playing. A neighbourhood kafana is where you go once you know the rhythm and want the everyday version. Specific named venues in either category change over time, so treat any recommendation as indicative.

## Costs and tipping

Prices are the most perishable thing on this page, so take the following as indicative only and check current menus. A full kafana meal for two — shared starters, grilled meat, sides and drinks — at a mid-range central or Skadarlija kafana has commonly been reported around 4,000–8,000 RSD. That figure is triangulated from menus and diner reports as of 2024–2025, not a fixed tariff.

For service, tipping around 5–10% for good service is reported as customary, often simply by rounding up and usually in cash. Remember that the musicians are tipped separately and directly — that is not covered by the service tip, and it is an indicative norm as of 2026. At the touristed kafanas, some visitors are surprised by music charges or higher prices on live-music nights; the fair way to think about these is as the cost of a special experience rather than everyday dining.

## Practical notes for foreigners

Language divides roughly along the same showpiece-versus-neighbourhood line. Showpiece kafanas have multilingual menus and English-capable staff, while neighbourhood kafanas may be Serbian-only with limited English. If you want the easier evening, start in Skadarlija; if you want the local one, be ready to point, smile and trust the kitchen.

A kafana evening is one of the most rewarding ways to spend a night in the city, and it pairs naturally with the rest of what there is to do — see our guide to [things to do in Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/things-to-do-in-belgrade) for how it fits into a wider stay. Go hungry, go unhurried, and let the meal find its own length.
