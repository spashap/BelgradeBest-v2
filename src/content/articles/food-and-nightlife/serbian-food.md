## What Serbian cuisine actually is

Serbian cuisine is hearty and meat-forward, built on a mix of Ottoman, Central-European and Balkan influences. Meat, pastry and bread, and local vegetables and dairy dominate the table, and meals are typically long and social. If you understand a handful of core dishes and the spreads that accompany them, you'll read almost any menu in the city with confidence.

This page explains the food itself. When you're ready to find out where to eat it, the [best restaurants in Belgrade](/food-and-nightlife/best-restaurants-in-belgrade) and the [Belgrade grill and street food](/food-and-nightlife/belgrade-grill-and-street-food) guides do the venue work.

## The core savoury dishes

A few dishes form the backbone of Serbian eating, and you'll meet them everywhere.

**Ćevapi** (also called ćevapčići) are small grilled minced-meat sausages — beef, pork, lamb or a mix — served with flatbread, onions and accompaniments like kajmak and ajvar. They are the quintessential grill order.

**Pljeskavica** is a large grilled minced-meat patty, the Serbian relative of a burger. The Leskovac style is a well-known spicy variant.

**Karađorđeva šnicla** is a Serbian signature: a rolled, breaded and fried veal or pork escalope stuffed with kajmak, often served with tartar sauce.

**Sarma** is cabbage rolls — fermented or pickled cabbage leaves stuffed with minced meat and rice, then slow-cooked. It's a winter and special-occasion dish.

**Pečenje** is roasted meat: whole-roasted pork, lamb or goat, central to celebrations such as the slava (a family's patron-saint day) and weddings.

## Staples, spreads and breads

Two spreads define Serbian food more than any single dish. **Kajmak** is a clotted-cream dairy spread, rich and mild; **ajvar** is a roasted red-pepper relish. Together they accompany grilled meats, breads and almost everything else. From the south comes **urnebes**, a spicy cheese-and-pepper salad and spread.

The bakery is its own institution. **Burek** is a flaky filled pastry — meat, cheese and other fillings — eaten with yoghurt and treated as a proper meal in its own right. **Gibanica** is a cheese-and-egg layered pastry. **Komplet lepinja** is a rich western-Serbian flatbread dish built with kajmak, gravy and egg.

For something fresh on the side, **shopska salad** — tomato, cucumber, pepper and onion, topped with grated white cheese — is the ubiquitous vegetable plate across the Balkans, and a reliable order at any table.

## Sweets and the pastry shop

Serbian dessert culture sits at a crossroads. There are Ottoman-influenced sweets such as baklava, and Austro-Hungarian-style cakes and custards such as krempita. Both are served in the poslastičarnica — the pastry-shop tradition that gives sweets their own dedicated space, separate from the grill and the kafana.

## Drinks at the table

**Rakija** — fruit brandy, most iconically šljivovica (plum) — is central to Serbian hospitality and table culture. It's drunk before, during and after meals, and offering it is a gesture of welcome. The traditional everyday coffee is **Turkish-style coffee**, served strong and unhurried.

## How Serbians eat

Meals are long and communal. The **kafana** — the traditional tavern — is the emblematic social eating-and-drinking space, where food and conversation stretch across hours. Dishes are often shared from central platters rather than plated individually, so eating becomes something you do together rather than alone.

Serbian food is widely described as very affordable by European-capital standards, with generous portions, framing the city as an underrated, good-value food destination (indicative, as of 2026). That value is part of why long, shared meals feel so natural here.

## Practical notes for foreigners

Vegetarians can eat in Belgrade, but it's worth knowing the cuisine is meat-centred, and cheese and animal fats are common. Some dishes are accidentally plant-friendly — prebranac (beans), posna or fasting versions of dishes, and the salads — but it's wise to clarify ingredients before ordering. Strict vegans should plan ahead rather than assume.

Beyond that, the best approach is simply to lean into how the food is meant to be eaten: order a few things, put the spreads in the middle, take your time, and let the meal run long. That's the heart of Serbian food culture, and it travels well to any table you sit down at.
