## The shortlist, and why it earns its place

A city's must-see list is usually a list of buildings. Belgrade's is more interesting as a list of *reasons*: each anchor stop opens a different window onto how the city works and where it has been. The canonical first-timer set is small and stable — Belgrade Fortress (Kalemegdan), the Temple of St. Sava, the Nikola Tesla Museum, Skadarlija, Republic Square with Knez Mihailova, Ada Ciganlija, Zemun with the Gardoš Tower, and Avala Tower just outside the city. These recur across essentially every reputable guide, so the list itself is not where the value lies. What follows is the interpretation: what each place is for, and whether it is worth your limited time.

## Belgrade Fortress (Kalemegdan)

If you see only one thing, see this. The fortress sits on the promontory above the meeting of the Sava and the Danube, with layered fortifications running from Roman through to Austrian periods — which is precisely why it matters. It is the one place where you can physically read Belgrade's whole contested history in stone, and it offers the best confluence panorama in the city. The grounds are open around the clock and free to enter, so it costs you nothing but the walk. The detail you'll actually need to plan a visit — seasonal hours for the interior sites, per-site tickets, and the question of whether a combined ticket is worth it — lives on our [understanding Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/understanding-belgrade) page rather than here.

## Temple of St. Sava

One of the largest Orthodox churches in the world, St. Sava is the anchor of modern Serbian Orthodox identity, and it reads that way: a vast marble façade, a mosaic interior, and an enormous dome built to impress. As of 2026 it is open daily from roughly 08:00 to 20:00, with free entry and a donation box. Treat those hours as indicative — they are the sort of detail that drifts, so confirm if you're cutting it fine. The point of going is the scale of the interior, which photographs poorly and rewards standing inside it.

## Nikola Tesla Museum

This is Belgrade's canonical science stop, holding Tesla's archive alongside live electrical demonstrations that make the visit more than a cabinet of papers. As of 2026 it opens Monday 10:00–18:00 and Tuesday to Sunday 10:00–20:00, with an English guided tour at around 800 RSD; tours run roughly hourly and last about 45 minutes. One practical warning worth repeating: it is cash only, with no cards accepted, so bring dinars. The fuller museum detail sits on our [Belgrade museums](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-museums) page.

## Skadarlija

Skadarlija is the cobblestone bohemian quarter of kafanas and live music, reported as "Belgrade's Montmartre" — a description to take with a pinch of salt, but a fair pointer to the mood. It is pedestrian and effectively always open, with individual venues setting their own hours. Its real use to a first-time visitor is as the easiest way into "old Belgrade" through food and atmosphere rather than through a ticket queue.

## Republic Square and Knez Mihailova

Republic Square is the city's main square — home to the National Museum, the National Theatre and the statue of Prince Mihailo — and Knez Mihailova is the pedestrian promenade running from it down towards the fortress. Both are public spaces, open at all hours. This is the everyday civic stage of Belgrade, the place where the city does its strolling, and it conveniently strings the central sights together on foot.

## Ada Ciganlija

Locals call Ada Ciganlija "Belgrade's sea": a lake and beach peninsula on the Sava with paths, cafés and sports facilities. It is a free-access city park, effectively open around the clock, with facilities and lifeguards active from May to September. One correction worth flagging, because it trips people up: the 07:30–15:30 hours listed on the official Ada site are office hours, not park access — you are not locked out in the afternoon. Ada is where you see how Belgraders actually use their rivers in summer.

## Zemun and the Gardoš Tower

Zemun is the Austro-Hungarian river town now folded into Belgrade, with Danube views and the signature Gardoš Tower crowning the hill. The character is noticeably different from the centre — lower, older, more Central European. We are deliberately not quoting fixed tower hours here, because the sources conflict on them; check the dedicated Zemun page before you climb.

## Avala Tower

Avala is a half-day escape: a TV-tower viewpoint on a low mountain roughly 16 km south of the city, paired with the Monument to the Unknown Hero, giving you a regional-landscape view rather than another cityscape. Its hours are genuinely uncertain. One business directory lists 09:00–18:00, while Apple Maps and Belgrade-Beat report 09:00–20:00. Treat 09:00–18:00 as the conservative baseline and the longer summer hours as reported but unconfirmed, and verify by phone or through the tourist organisation before making the trip. Ticket prices are reported at around 200/100 RSD, with a VIP option near 400 RSD, but these are not officially confirmed for 2026 — indicative only.

## How it all fits together

The encouraging news for planning is that most of this clusters tightly. Guides broadly agree that the central core — Republic Square to Knez Mihailova to Kalemegdan, plus Skadarlija — is walkable as a single loop, with Vračar (St. Sava and Tesla) a short hop away, and Zemun, Ada and Avala sitting naturally as half-day add-ons. Since January 2025, public transport has been free, so moving between these areas carries no fare — a small but real simplification for first-timers. If you want this turned into an actual day-by-day plan, our [Belgrade in 3 days](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-itinerary-3-days) itinerary does that work.

A closing note on honesty: the hours and prices above are accurate as of 2026 but perishable by nature, and the Avala figures in particular should be confirmed rather than trusted. The list of what to see is stable; the fine print around it is not.
