## The shortlist, and why it earns its place

A city's must-see list is usually a list of buildings. Belgrade's is more interesting as a list of *reasons*: each anchor stop opens a different window onto how the city works and where it has been. The essential things to do in Belgrade are a small, stable set: Belgrade Fortress (Kalemegdan), the Temple of St. Sava, the Nikola Tesla Museum, Skadarlija, Republic Square with Knez Mihailova, Ada Ciganlija, Zemun with the Gardoš Tower, and Avala Tower just outside the city. They recur across essentially every reputable guide, so the list itself is not where the value lies. What follows is the interpretation an outsider actually needs — what each place is *for*, where it sits in the city, and whether it's worth your limited time. The good news for planning: most of the central ones are within walking distance of each other, which is why Belgrade rewards a first visit on foot.

## Belgrade Fortress (Kalemegdan)

If you see only one thing, see this. The fortress sits on the promontory in **Stari Grad** (the old town) where the Sava meets the Danube, about a 10–15 minute walk northwest of Republic Square along the Knez Mihailova promenade. Its layered fortifications run from Roman through to Austrian periods — which is precisely why it matters: it's the one place where you can physically read Belgrade's whole contested history in stone, the same strategic high ground that has been besieged and rebuilt by everyone who ever wanted the rivers below. It also has the best confluence panorama in the city, the view down onto the Sava–Danube junction that explains in a glance why Belgrade is where it is. The grounds (and the surrounding Kalemegdan park) are open around the clock and free to enter, so it costs you nothing but the walk — you can wander the ramparts at any hour. The detail you'll need to plan a visit — seasonal hours for the interior sites and museums, their per-site tickets, and whether a combined ticket is worth it — lives on our [understanding Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/understanding-belgrade) page rather than here.

## Temple of St. Sava

One of the largest Orthodox churches in the world, St. Sava anchors the **Vračar** district, roughly 2 km south of Republic Square — a 25–30 minute walk, or a short ride if you'd rather save your legs. It's the anchor of modern Serbian Orthodox identity, and it reads that way: a vast white-marble façade, a glittering mosaic interior, and an enormous dome built to impress rather than to console. That scale is the point — it's deliberately monumental, a national statement in stone as much as a place of worship. As of 2026 it's open daily from roughly 08:00 to 20:00, with free entry and a donation box. Treat those hours as indicative — they're the sort of detail that drifts, so confirm if you're cutting it fine. The reason to go is the interior, which photographs poorly and rewards standing inside it; the sheer volume of the space doesn't survive a screen. It pairs naturally with the Tesla Museum, which is in the same part of town.

## Nikola Tesla Museum

Belgrade's canonical science stop is also in **Vračar**, near Slavija square and a short walk from St. Sava, so the two make an easy pair. It holds Tesla's personal archive — papers, instruments and his ashes in a golden orb — but what lifts it above a cabinet of documents is the live electrical demonstrations, where staff fire up working models so you feel the science rather than just read about it. As of 2026 it opens Monday 10:00–18:00 and Tuesday to Sunday 10:00–20:00, with an English guided tour at around 800 RSD; tours run roughly hourly and last about 45 minutes, and the demonstrations are the part worth timing your visit around. One practical warning worth repeating, because it catches people out: it's cash only, no cards, so bring dinars. The fuller museum detail — and how it fits a wider museum day — sits on our [Belgrade museums](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-museums) page.

## Skadarlija

Skadarlija is the cobblestone bohemian quarter of *kafanas* (traditional taverns) and live music, tucked into **Stari Grad** just a few minutes' walk east of Republic Square, on the way toward Dorćol. It's reported as "Belgrade's Montmartre" — a description to take with a pinch of salt, but a fair pointer to the mood: a short, sloping pedestrian street where the bohemian-artist past is now mostly a dining-and-music experience. The street is pedestrian and effectively always open, with individual venues setting their own hours; it comes alive in the evening, when the kafanas put musicians out and the tables fill. Its real use to a first-time visitor is as the easiest way into "old Belgrade" through food and atmosphere rather than through a ticket queue, and it sits close enough to the central loop to fold into the same day — most people walk over for dinner after a day on foot in the centre.

## Republic Square and Knez Mihailova

Republic Square is the city's main square and natural orientation point — home to the National Museum, the National Theatre and the statue of Prince Mihailo — and **Knez Mihailova** is the pedestrian promenade running northwest from it down toward the fortress. Both are in **Stari Grad**, public, and open at all hours. This is the everyday civic stage of Belgrade, the place where the city does its strolling, and it conveniently strings the central sights together: stand here and the fortress, Skadarlija and the shopping street are all a short walk away. For most visitors this square is where a day in Belgrade begins.

## Ada Ciganlija

Locals call Ada Ciganlija "Belgrade's sea": a lake and beach peninsula on the **Sava**, southwest of the centre, reachable by a short bus ride or bike rather than on foot. It's a free-access city park with swimming, cafés and bars, cycling and running paths, and sports facilities from kayaking to bungee jumping — effectively open around the clock, with the beach facilities and lifeguards active from May to September. One correction worth flagging, because it trips people up: the 07:30–15:30 hours listed on the official Ada site are administrative office hours, not park access — the peninsula doesn't close on you in the afternoon, and you can stay through the evening. Ada is the single best place to see how Belgraders actually use their rivers in summer, which also means it's at its best in warm weather and fairly quiet out of season.

## Zemun and the Gardoš Tower

Zemun is the Austro-Hungarian river town now folded into Belgrade, sitting upriver along the **Danube** about 6 km northwest of the centre — close enough for a half-day, far enough to feel like somewhere else. For centuries it was literally a different country — the Habsburg frontier across the river from Ottoman Belgrade — and that history is the reason it still looks and feels distinct: lower, older, more Central European, with pastel houses, a fish-restaurant quay along the Danube and the signature **Gardoš Tower** crowning the hill for the view back over the water. Climbing the tower is the usual goal, but we're deliberately not quoting fixed hours here, because the sources conflict on them; check the dedicated [Zemun](/visit-belgrade/zemun) page before you go up.

## Avala Tower

Avala is the half-day escape that leaves the city behind: a TV-tower viewpoint on a low mountain roughly 16 km south of Belgrade, paired with the Monument to the Unknown Hero (a Meštrović work that is a landmark in its own right). The reason to make the trip is that it swaps the cityscape for a regional one — green hills and the country rolling away from the observation deck — so it's a different kind of view from the fortress, not a repeat of it. Its hours are genuinely uncertain, so this is one to confirm before you commit a half-day to it: one business directory lists 09:00–18:00, while Apple Maps and Belgrade-Beat report 09:00–20:00. Treat 09:00–18:00 as the conservative baseline and the longer summer hours as reported but unconfirmed, and verify by phone or through the tourist organisation before making the trip. Ticket prices are reported at around 200/100 RSD (full/reduced), with a VIP option near 400 RSD, but these aren't officially confirmed for 2026 — indicative only.

## How it all fits together

The encouraging news for planning is that most of this clusters tightly. The central core — Republic Square to Knez Mihailova to Kalemegdan, plus Skadarlija — is walkable as a single loop, well under a kilometre end to end, with Vračar (St. Sava and the Tesla Museum) a short walk or ride south. Zemun, Ada and Avala sit naturally as half-day add-ons in three different directions: Zemun upriver to the northwest, Ada southwest on the Sava, Avala south out of town. Since January 2025 public transport has been free, so moving between these areas carries no fare — a small but real simplification for first-timers.
