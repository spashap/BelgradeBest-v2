## How long to stay

The consensus across independent itineraries and guides is that at least three days is ideal to see Belgrade's main sights plus a couple of neighbourhoods, with compressed one- and two-day options also common. Three days lets you add a few quieter corners beyond the centre rather than rushing the headline sites. If your time is shorter, the same plan compresses cleanly:

- **One day** covers the central spine on foot — Republic Square → Knez Mihailova → Belgrade Fortress → [Skadarlija](/glossary/skadarlija), all within walking distance.
- **Two days** keeps that first day intact and adds a neighbourhood and a museum (Vračar for the Temple of St. Sava and the Nikola Tesla Museum).
- **Three days** adds a half-day escape on top — Zemun, [Ada Ciganlija](/glossary/ada-ciganlija) or Avala — or a full day trip out of the city.

The detailed day-by-day plans for each of these lengths are below; the short version is that nothing is wasted as you scale up or down, because each longer plan just keeps the shorter one and bolts more on.

This page is about sequencing and logistics — the *why* and the deeper background live on the spine pages. For a wider sense of what the city is and how it fits together, [understanding Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/understanding-belgrade) is the place to start, and the full menu of sights sits under [things to do in Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/things-to-do-in-belgrade).

## How the city clusters

Belgrade's central sights cluster tightly enough to walk between, which is what makes a day-by-day plan work. The old-town core sits in the Stari Grad district on the right bank of the Sava and Danube. Republic Square (Trg Republike) is the orientation point most people start from; from there, Knez Mihailova — the pedestrian shopping spine — runs roughly north-west, and at its far end Belgrade Fortress ([Kalemegdan](/glossary/kalemegdan)) crowns the ridge above the river confluence. The fortress is about 0.9 km from Republic Square and 0.6–0.8 km from Knez Mihailova, so the whole first-day spine is a comfortable on-foot loop rather than a transit problem. Skadarlija, the cobbled [kafana](/glossary/kafana) quarter, lies just east of the square — a 5–8 minute downhill walk — which is why it slots naturally into the same day as a dinner stop.

The next ring out is still close. Dorćol, a mixed inner-city neighbourhood good for cafes and a more local feel, is a 10–15 minute walk north-east of Republic Square toward the Danube; Savamala, the riverfront nightlife and creative district, is about 0.8–1.0 km south-west of the square, downhill toward the Sava. Vračar — home to the Temple of St. Sava and the Nikola Tesla Museum — sits south-east of the core, generally a short hop from the centre (these sights are detailed on their canonical pages rather than mapped here). Zemun, Ada Ciganlija and Avala are half-day add-ons: far enough out to want transport, close enough to bolt onto any day.

## Day 1 — the old-town spine

Start at Republic Square, the meeting point locals call "at the horse" (the Prince Mihailo statue), with the National Museum and National Theatre framing it. Walk Knez Mihailova end to end — cafés, buskers, a century of façades — and let it deliver you to Kalemegdan. The fortress grounds are free and open around the clock; budget a couple of unhurried hours for the walls, the park and the confluence panorama, and note that the ticketed interior sights (the Roman Well, Nebojša Tower and others) run seasonal hours with small entry fees — reported around 80–300 RSD each, or roughly 450 RSD combined, as of 2026.

If you'd rather be walked than wander, this exact spine is what nearly every free walking tour covers: Republic Square → Knez Mihailova → Kalemegdan → Skadarlija, about 2–2.5 hours, tip-based at roughly €10–20 (reported, as of 2026) — a genuinely efficient Day 1 orientation. Either way, finish in Skadarlija: the cobbled kafana quarter is at its best in the evening, and a long dinner with live music there is the classic first Belgrade night.

## Day 2 — Vračar, a museum, and the modern city

Day 2 moves one ring out, and still barely needs transport. Morning in Vračar: the Temple of St. Sava — one of the largest Orthodox churches in the world — is free to enter and generally open around 08:00–20:00 (reported, as of 2026), and the mosaic interior justifies the detour alone. From there it's a short walk to the Nikola Tesla Museum, which runs timed, hourly guided tours (in English among others) with live electrical demonstrations; tickets are reported around 800 RSD and cash-only (as of 2025) — arrive shortly before the hour.

The afternoon is a fork. Option one: a museum from the [Belgrade museums](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-museums) shortlist — the National Museum back on Republic Square is the natural pick if you skipped it on Day 1. Option two, and the more distinctive choice: cross the Sava for a New Belgrade half-day — the planned socialist city of blocks, the Genex Tower and the concrete monuments, walkable in a few focused hours with our [brutalist Belgrade guide](/visit-belgrade/brutalist-belgrade) — the fastest way to see the *other* Belgrade, and most visitors skip it.

## Day 3 — Zemun, the lake, or out of town

Three good shapes for the last day, in rising order of ambition:

- **Zemun.** The former Austro-Hungarian town on the Danube — old streets, Gardoš hill and its tower, fish restaurants on the quay — makes an easy, rewarding half-day; the full picture is in our [Zemun guide](/visit-belgrade/zemun). Pair it with a slow afternoon back in Dorćol.
- **Ada Ciganlija.** In the warmer months (facilities run roughly May–September), Belgrade's lake-beach peninsula is where the city actually spends its summer days — a half-day of swimming, paths and lakeside cafés, and free to enter.
- **A day trip.** Novi Sad is the standard add-on and self-guided-friendly — about 32–36 minutes by high-speed train (reported, as of 2026) — while the combined Novi Sad + Sremski Karlovci + Fruška Gora monasteries loop, the Iron Gates gorge and Oplenac/Topola are better done as organised tours. The comparisons and logistics live in [Belgrade day trips](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-day-trips).

## Practical notes for planning

**Getting around:** the central core is best on foot — that's the whole design of Days 1 and 2 — and for the longer hops, public transport (buses, trams, trolleybuses) has been free for all passengers since 1 January 2025 (policy current as of 2026; worth re-checking closer to travel). **When to come:** April–June and September–October are the best months — mild, lively, and clear of the July–August heat. **The 2027 caveat:** Expo 2027 runs 15 May–15 August 2027 and will intensify that summer's crowds and prices, so flexible travellers that year should lean into the shoulder months. **Budget:** Belgrade is affordable but no longer ultra-cheap — rough daily bands reported as of 2026 run about $50 (budget), $150–170 (mid-range) and $250–450+ (comfort and up); the fuller value question is argued out in [is Belgrade worth visiting](/visit-belgrade/is-belgrade-worth-visiting).
