## Why districts come first

Before you memorise a single tram route, it helps to grasp that Belgrade's districts each read almost like separate cities, with a distinct personality and price point, and that locals use district names constantly for orientation. Get the map of characters straight in your head and the rest of the city falls into place. This page is an orientation to those characters rather than a hotel or nightlife list; for the wider context of how the city fits together, see [understanding Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/understanding-belgrade).

## Stari Grad — the historic core

Stari Grad, the Old Town, is the central historic municipality, gathering Republic Square, Knez Mihailova, Kalemegdan and parts of Dorćol and Skadarlija into a dense, walkable grid. Its buildings mix Habsburg-era, interwar and socialist styles within a few streets of each other, which is part of the pleasure of wandering it. For most first-timers this is the natural base, and it is the main node for the city's free walking tours. If you only have a short stay, Stari Grad is where the greatest number of sights sit within comfortable walking distance.

## Dorćol — eclectic and close to the river

Dorćol is one of the most eclectic central districts, and it is worth knowing that locals split it in two. The "upper" part is the more upscale half, while "lower" Dorćol is the creative, up-and-coming stretch nearer the Danube. The district sits close to the river, so the natural way to use it as a visitor is café, bar and small-gallery hunting, finished off along the Danube promenade. It rewards slow, unstructured walking more than a checklist.

### Skadarlija — the bohemian street

Within the Dorćol and Stari Grad area runs Skadarlija, a short cobblestone street long framed as Belgrade's bohemian quarter, known for its kafanas and live music. Some 2025 articles describe it as the city's second-most-visited spot after Kalemegdan, but that ranking is best read as a marketing claim rather than a measured fact. The bohemian character, on the other hand, is real enough to plan an evening around.

## Savamala — warehouses and transformation

Savamala is a historic warehouse and industrial district on the Sava, partly redeveloped, and reported as a place known for street art, creative spaces and clubs. In recent years it has increasingly been folded into the "Belgrade Waterfront" narrative. The most rewarding way to experience it is on foot: walking between the old Savamala streets, the riverside barges and the newer Waterfront promenades lets you read the city's ongoing transformation in a single stretch.

## Vračar — St. Sava and quieter streets

Vračar is a central district anchored by the Temple of St. Sava and the National Library, with cafés and quieter residential streets. The Nikola Tesla Museum is also here, which makes the district easy to plan: you can combine St. Sava, the Tesla Museum and a café stop into a comfortable half-day without needing transport between them. It feels more residential and unhurried than the Old Town, a useful contrast if Stari Grad starts to feel busy.

## Zemun — the old soul across the water

Zemun is a former Austro-Hungarian frontier town that is now a Belgrade municipality, and it still feels distinct from the centre. It preserves 18th- and 19th-century streets, a riverside quay and Gardoš hill, and is often described as the city's "old soul." The change in atmosphere as you arrive is noticeable, and it is enough of a place in its own right that we give it a [dedicated Zemun guide](/visit-belgrade/zemun) rather than trying to compress it here.

## New Belgrade — the planned socialist city

Across the Sava lies New Belgrade, Novi Beograd, the planned socialist city of large residential blocks, wide boulevards, offices and big venues such as Genex, the Sava Centre and Palata Srbija. It functions as an open-air museum of socialist modernism, and it looks and feels nothing like the historic core. If that architecture is what draws you, the detail lives on our [brutalist Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/brutalist-belgrade) page, which treats the district's buildings properly.

## How to use these characters

Think of the districts as a set of moods rather than a route. Stari Grad for the dense historic centre, Dorćol for cafés and the Danube, Skadarlija for an old-fashioned evening, Savamala for the city's industrial reinvention, Vračar for St. Sava and a calmer pace, Zemun for its frontier-town atmosphere, and New Belgrade for socialist modernism. Once the characters are clear, you can plan days that flow naturally between them. This page deliberately stays an orientation hub: it points you toward the deeper guides rather than duplicating their detail, and it leaves hotels and nightlife to other parts of the site.
