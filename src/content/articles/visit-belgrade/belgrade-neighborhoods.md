## Why districts come first

Before you memorise a single tram route, it helps to grasp that Belgrade's districts each read almost like separate cities, with a distinct personality and price point, and that locals use district names constantly for orientation. Belgrade is a large city, and Dorćol, Vračar, Zemun, New Belgrade and the rest each carry their own atmosphere; get the map of characters straight in your head and the rest of the city falls into place. This page is an orientation to those characters — what each district feels like and what there is to see in it. It is for the visitor who wants to understand the city before booking anything; when you get to the booking stage, our [area-by-area guides on where to stay](/areas) weigh each district as a base. For the wider context of how the city fits together, see [understanding Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/understanding-belgrade).

A quick way to hold the whole picture: almost everything historic sits on the right (south) bank of the Sava and Danube, gathered around the old core; the planned modern city and the old frontier town sit across the water on the left bank. Once you know which side of the river you are on, the districts arrange themselves naturally.

## Stari Grad — the historic core

Stari Grad, the Old Town, is the central municipality on the right bank, covering the historic core between the Sava–Danube confluence and the Terazije ridge. It gathers Republic Square — the busy central square at the eastern end of Knez Mihailova — together with the Knez Mihailova pedestrian street, the Belgrade Fortress at [Kalemegdan](/glossary/kalemegdan), and parts of Dorćol and [Skadarlija](/glossary/skadarlija) into a dense, walkable grid. It borders Dorćol, Skadarlija, Vračar, Savski Venac and both riverfronts.

What gives the district its texture is the way its buildings mix Habsburg-era, interwar and socialist styles within a few streets of each other, which is part of the pleasure of wandering it — you cross a century of architecture in a single block. Its three set-pieces frame everything else: Republic Square, with the National Museum and the National Theatre, is the natural meeting point and the place locals say "at the horse" (the Prince Mihailo statue); Knez Mihailova is the broad pedestrian spine of cafés, shops and street musicians running from the square down toward the fortress; and Kalemegdan, the Belgrade Fortress and its park at the confluence, closes the walk with the city's best river views. (The fortress's 2,300-year layered history is a story in itself — it anchors the understanding-Belgrade walk rather than this page.) Stari Grad is also the main node for free walking tours, nearly all of which run the Republic Square → Knez Mihailova → Kalemegdan spine. As a place to sleep it's the default first-timer choice, with the trade-offs covered in the [Stari Grad area guide](/areas/stari-grad).

## Dorćol — the eclectic inner city

Dorćol lies immediately north-east of Republic Square, sloping toward the Danube — a 10–15 minute walk from the square. It is really two districts wearing one name, divided roughly along Cara Dušana street: **upper Dorćol**, closer to the centre, more polished and upscale; and **lower Dorćol**, nearer the river, creative and up-and-coming, with a quieter residential grid and riverfront paths — upper for café and bar hunting among small galleries, lower for an unhurried walk toward the Danube promenade.

Dorćol's sight with the deepest history is easy to miss: the Bajrakli Mosque, the only survivor of the 200-plus mosques Belgrade had under Ottoman rule. Otherwise the district's appeal is everyday life rather than set-pieces — this is where central Belgrade feels most local while staying within walking distance of everything. Considering it as a base? The [Dorćol area guide](/areas/dorcol) covers that side.

## Skadarlija — the bohemian quarter

Skadarlija is tiny — essentially one cobbled street, Skadarska, and its surroundings — a 5–8 minute downhill walk east of Republic Square. It preserves a late-19th/early-20th-century bohemian quarter of [kafanas](/glossary/kafana), courtyards and street art, and evenings here mean live music and long traditional dinners. Some 2025 coverage calls it the city's second-most-visited spot after Kalemegdan; treat the ranking as a marketing claim, but the bohemian character is real. By day it's a ten-minute atmosphere-and-photos stroll; by night it's a destination. If its charm tempts you to sleep there, the [Skadarlija area guide](/areas/skadarlija) weighs the cobbles-and-late-noise trade-off honestly.

## Savamala — the riverfront in transition

Savamala sits on the right bank of the Sava, about 0.8–1.0 km south-west of Republic Square, downhill between Branko's bridge and the old port. A former warehouse and industrial district, it became Belgrade's creative quarter — street art, clubs, converted spaces — and is now being folded into the glass-and-steel Belgrade Waterfront redevelopment. That collision is exactly why it's worth a walk: within a few hundred metres you read the city's whole current transformation, old warehouses against new towers. Nightlife is the district's other identity, covered on its own pages; the base-or-not question lives in the [Savamala area guide](/areas/savamala).

## Vračar — the temple and the quiet streets

Vračar is the residential district south-east of the core, anchored by the Temple of St Sava — one of the largest Orthodox churches in the world, its white marble and mosaic dome visible across the city — together with the National Library beside it and the Nikola Tesla Museum a few streets away. Around the set-pieces run café-lined, genuinely liveable streets. The natural visitor use is a half-day: St Sava, the Tesla Museum, then coffee. As a base it trades a little distance for calm — see the [Vračar area guide](/areas/vracar).

## Zemun — the other town

Zemun, north-west along the Danube beyond New Belgrade, was a separate Austro-Hungarian frontier town — Habsburg from 1717 to 1918, staring at Ottoman Belgrade across the water — and it has never fully stopped being one. It preserves 18th- and 19th-century streets, a riverside quay, and Gardoš hill topped by the 1896 Millennium Tower, with a gentler, more provincial rhythm than anywhere in central Belgrade — which is why it's often called the city's "old soul." It deserves its own half-day at minimum; the full story is in our [Zemun guide](/visit-belgrade/zemun), and the staying-there angle in the [Zemun area guide](/areas/zemun).

## New Belgrade — the planned city

New Belgrade (Novi Beograd), across the Sava on the left bank, is the counter-argument to everything above: a planned socialist city, built from 1948, of numbered residential blocks (*blokovi*), wide boulevards and monumental structures — the Genex Tower (1977–80), the Sava Centre, Palata Srbija. It functions as an open-air museum of socialist modernism, and reading it properly is a half-day walk of its own, mapped in our [brutalist Belgrade guide](/visit-belgrade/brutalist-belgrade). Character-wise it is the opposite of Stari Grad, and seeing both is the fastest way to understand what the 20th century did to this city. Business travellers often end up based here; the [Novi Beograd area guide](/areas/novi-beograd) explains when that makes sense.
