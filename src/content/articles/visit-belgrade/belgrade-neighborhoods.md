## Why districts come first

Before you memorise a single tram route, it helps to grasp that Belgrade's districts each read almost like separate cities, with a distinct personality and price point, and that locals use district names constantly for orientation. Belgrade is a large city, and Dorćol, Vračar, Zemun, New Belgrade and the rest each carry their own atmosphere; get the map of characters straight in your head and the rest of the city falls into place. This page is an orientation to those characters rather than a hotel or nightlife list — it is for the visitor who wants to understand the city before booking anything. For the wider context of how the city fits together, see [understanding Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/understanding-belgrade).

A quick way to hold the whole picture: almost everything historic sits on the right (south) bank of the Sava and Danube, gathered around the old core; the planned modern city and the old frontier town sit across the water on the left bank. Once you know which side of the river you are on, the districts arrange themselves naturally.

## Stari Grad — the historic core

Stari Grad, the Old Town, is the central municipality on the right bank, covering the historic core between the Sava–Danube confluence and the Terazije ridge. It gathers Republic Square — the busy central square at the eastern end of Knez Mihailova — together with the Knez Mihailova pedestrian street, the Belgrade Fortress at Kalemegdan, and parts of Dorćol and Skadarlija into a dense, walkable grid. It borders Dorćol, Skadarlija, Vračar, Savski Venac and both riverfronts.

What gives the district its texture is the way its buildings mix Habsburg-era, interwar and socialist styles within a few streets of each other, which is part of the pleasure of wandering it — you cross a century of architecture in a single block. Its three set-pieces frame everything else: Republic Square, with the National Museum and the National Theatre, is the natural meeting point and the place locals say "at the horse" (the Prince Mihailo statue); Knez Mihailova is the broad pedestrian spine of cafés, shops and street musicians running from the square down toward the fortress; and Kalemegdan, the Belgrade Fortress and its park at the confluence, closes the walk with the city's best river views. (The fortress itself gets fuller treatment elsewhere on the site — here it is simply the anchor of the Old Town.)

For most first-timers this is the natural base, and it is the main node for the city's free walking tours: from Republic Square you can reach the fortress, the shopping street and the old lanes on foot. If you only have a short stay, Stari Grad is where the greatest number of sights sit within comfortable walking distance, and where the city feels most concentrated and most itself.

## Dorćol — eclectic and close to the river

Dorćol is one of the most eclectic central districts, sitting immediately north and north-east of Republic Square within Stari Grad and sloping down toward the Danube. The name itself, from a Turkish word for a crossroads, hints at its long history as a mixed, trading quarter, and that mixed character survives: it is residential and lived-in, not a sights district, which is exactly why visitors who want to feel the everyday city gravitate to it.

It is worth knowing that locals split it in two, with Cara Dušana the rough boundary. "Upper" Dorćol — the area closer to the centre, above Cara Dušana — is the more upscale, mixed-residential half, with cafés, small shops, wine bars and cultural venues around streets like Dobračina and Visokog Stevana; it is roughly a ten-to-fifteen-minute walk from Republic Square and reads as a polished, slightly bohemian extension of the Old Town. "Lower" Dorćol is the creative, up-and-coming stretch: it slopes below Cara Dušana toward the Danube and the lower slopes of the fortress, a quieter, more residential grid of older low-rise and newer infill nearer the river, with a younger, scrappier energy and a steady turnover of new cafés and small galleries.

The natural way to use the district as a visitor is café, bar and small-gallery hunting, finished off along the Danube quay, where the riverside promenade and its summer bars draw crowds on warm evenings. Dorćol suits the traveller who has already ticked off the monuments and now wants to sit, watch and wander — it rewards slow, unstructured walking more than a checklist.

### Skadarlija — the bohemian street

Within the Dorćol and Stari Grad area, about a five-to-eight-minute walk east of Republic Square and just north-east of Terazije, runs Skadarlija — a short cobblestoned street built around Skadarska, long framed as Belgrade's bohemian quarter and known for its traditional kafanas and live music. It is the city's old artists' and writers' haunt, sometimes likened to a miniature Montmartre, and it preserves a late-19th- and early-20th-century ambience, with restaurant courtyards, strolling musicians, murals and small galleries along the way; it borders upper Dorćol and the Cetinjska area, another pocket of bars and yards just off Skadarska.

Some 2025 articles describe it as the city's second-most-visited spot after Kalemegdan, but that ranking is best read as a marketing claim rather than a measured fact. The bohemian character, on the other hand, is real enough to plan an evening around — this is the obvious choice for an old-fashioned night of grilled food, rakija and live tamburica music, touristy in places but genuinely atmospheric after dark.

## Savamala — warehouses and transformation

Savamala is a historic warehouse and industrial district in the Savski Venac municipality, on the right bank of the Sava just south-west of the old-town ridge — roughly under a kilometre south-west of Republic Square, stretching between the Branko bridge and the old Sava port. It borders Stari Grad above the slope, the Belgrade Waterfront zone and the Sava riverfront, and its spine is Karađorđeva Street, lined with the grand but long-faded façades of the city's first riverfront merchant quarter.

Partly redeveloped, it is reported as a place known for street art, creative spaces, design studios, riverfront clubs and the Sava barge (splav) scene, and over the last decade it became the city's emblem of post-industrial reinvention — a rough, graffiti-covered quarter that filled its old depots with galleries, bars and nightlife. In recent years it has increasingly been folded into the "Belgrade Waterfront" narrative, the large new-build development rising along the same riverbank, and the two faces now sit side by side. The most rewarding way to experience it is on foot: a short walk downhill from central Stari Grad lets you read the city's ongoing transformation in a single stretch, between the old Savamala streets, the riverside barges and the newer Waterfront promenades. It suits the visitor drawn to street art, edgier nightlife and the visible friction between old and new Belgrade.

## Vračar — St. Sava and quieter streets

Vračar is a central district just inland from Stari Grad, up the ridge from the river and away from the confluence — one of the smallest but densest and most sought-after residential municipalities, the kind of leafy, café-lined neighbourhood locals actually want to live in. It is anchored by the Temple of St. Sava, one of the largest Orthodox churches in the world and the city's most recognisable landmark, with the National Library beside it on the same plateau, and it is threaded with cafés and quieter residential streets.

The Nikola Tesla Museum is also here, which makes the district easy to plan: you can combine St. Sava, the Tesla Museum and a café stop into a comfortable half-day on foot without needing transport between them. It feels more residential and unhurried than the Old Town — a useful contrast if Stari Grad starts to feel busy — and it suits visitors who want a calmer base with real neighbourhood life and an easy walk back into the centre.

## Zemun — the old soul across the water

Zemun is a former Austro-Hungarian frontier town that is now a Belgrade municipality, lying north-west of the centre along the Danube, beyond New Belgrade, on the opposite side of the river from the old core. For centuries it stood on the very edge of the Habsburg Empire, facing Ottoman Belgrade across the water, and that separate history still shapes it: it preserves 18th- and 19th-century streets, a low-rise Central-European character, a riverside quay lined with fish restaurants, and Gardoš hill crowned by its Millennium (Sibinjanin Janko) Tower, and it is often described as the city's "old soul."

Because it grew up as a separate town under a different empire, the change in atmosphere as you arrive is noticeable — gentler, more provincial, more Mitteleuropa than Balkan — and it is enough of a place in its own right that we give it a [dedicated Zemun guide](/visit-belgrade/zemun) rather than trying to compress it here. It suits the visitor who wants a half-day escape from the big city without leaving it.

## New Belgrade — the planned socialist city

Across the Sava from the historic core lies New Belgrade, Novi Beograd, the planned socialist city laid out on the flat left bank in the second half of the twentieth century: large residential blocks (the famous numbered *blokovi*), wide boulevards, offices and big venues such as Genex (the "Western Gate" tower), the Sava Centre and Palata Srbija. Built largely from scratch on reclaimed marshland to house the new socialist capital, it functions as an open-air museum of socialist modernism, and it looks and feels nothing like the right-bank old town — it sits between the central districts and Zemun, and today doubles as the city's main business and conference district.

It is not a strolling-and-sights district in the Old Town sense; its appeal is the scale and ambition of its architecture and the everyday life of its blocks. If that architecture is what draws you, the detail lives on our [brutalist Belgrade](/visit-belgrade/brutalist-belgrade) page, which treats the district's buildings properly.

## How to use these characters

Think of the districts as a set of moods rather than a route. Stari Grad for the dense historic centre and the big sights, Dorćol for cafés and the Danube, Skadarlija for an old-fashioned evening, Savamala for the city's industrial reinvention and edgier nightlife, Vračar for St. Sava and a calmer residential pace, Zemun for its frontier-town atmosphere, and New Belgrade for socialist modernism and big venues. Most of these sit within walking distance of each other on the right bank, so you can chain two or three in a day and let the mood shift as you go; only New Belgrade and Zemun ask for a short tram or bus hop across the river.

Once the characters are clear, you can plan days that flow naturally between them. This page deliberately stays an orientation hub: it points you toward the deeper guides rather than duplicating their detail, and it leaves hotels and nightlife to other parts of the site.
