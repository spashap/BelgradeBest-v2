## Do you actually need a car in Belgrade?

Start with the honest answer, because it saves most visitors money and hassle: for Belgrade itself, a car is more liability than asset. The historic core is walkable, the city's buses, trams and trolleybuses have been **free to ride since 2025**, and taxis — or the CarGo and Yandex Go apps — are inexpensive for anything further. Against that, central parking is tightly regulated, time-limited and genuinely hard to find during working hours, and many older central apartments have no parking at all.

Where a rental genuinely pays off is **everything beyond the city**. Serbia and the wider Balkans open up enormously with your own wheels: the easy day trips to Novi Sad, the Fruška Gora monasteries and the wine town of Sremski Karlovci, plus longer road trips across the region. The clean rule of thumb: skip the car for your Belgrade days, and pick one up for the days you're leaving town. Our [day-trips guide](/visit-belgrade/belgrade-day-trips) covers where those drives lead.

## Where should you rent — airport or city, chain or local?

You have the same two-by-two choice as anywhere: international chain versus local agency, and airport versus city branch. The big international names — **Sixt, Avis, Hertz, Europcar and Enterprise** — operate alongside local Serbian rental firms, and both are present at **Nikola Tesla Airport** and at branches around the city.

Airport pickup is the most convenient if you want the car the moment you land, but airport locations often carry extra surcharges; a **city-centre branch** can work out cheaper if, following the advice above, you only need the car for the days you head out of town. The practical move is to **compare prices and terms across several providers** before committing rather than walking up to a single desk — and when you compare, read past the headline daily rate to the things that actually bite: the cross-border policy, the fuel rule, the excess/insurance terms, and any young-driver or one-way fees.

## What documents and conditions do you need?

The baseline requirements are standard but worth checking against your own situation:

- A **full driving licence**, typically held for **at least two years**. Some firms set a minimum age (often 21–23) and add a young-driver surcharge below a certain age.
- A **passport or ID**, and a **credit card** in the main driver's name for the deposit — debit cards and cash deposits are often refused.
- An **International Driving Permit** if your licence is printed in a **non-Latin alphabet** (Cyrillic, Arabic, Japanese and so on); in that case it's mandatory, not optional. Many EU, UK and US visitors can drive on their home licence, but carrying an IDP is a cheap hedge against a desk or roadside dispute.

Add any extra drivers to the contract at pickup — an unlisted driver usually voids the insurance if anything goes wrong.

## How does parking work if you do have a car?

This is the part that surprises people, so it's worth understanding before you drive into the centre. On-street parking in central Belgrade — inside the **number-2 circular tram route** — is split into **colour-coded zones with time limits**: a **red zone** (maximum 1 hour), a **yellow zone** (maximum 2 hours) and a **green zone** (maximum 3 hours). Charging applies **Monday to Friday** through the day (roughly 7am–9pm) and on **Saturday mornings**, while **Sundays and public holidays are free**.

The catch for visitors is payment. Locals pay by **SMS to a zone-specific number**, but that relies on a **Serbian SIM card** and frequently won't process on a foreign number — so it's not a reliable method for tourists. Enforcement, on the other hand, is very reliable: wardens patrol constantly, an unpaid ticket draws a fine (reported around 1,870 RSD), and a persistent offender can be towed by the "spider" tow truck. The stress-free answer is to skip on-street parking and use a **public parking garage**, which takes cash or card, needs no SIM or app, and gives you a guaranteed spot. If you're staying somewhere central with a car, book accommodation that includes **private parking or a garage** rather than counting on the street.

## Taking a rental across borders

If a road trip is the whole reason you're renting, sort the cross-border rules *before* you book, because they're stricter here than in much of Europe.

Standard rental insurance (the **Green Card**) covers most of Europe, but there are important exceptions and add-ons. For **North Macedonia, Albania** and a list of other countries, a Green Card endorsement plus a **cross-border permit** is required, so the company needs to prepare paperwork in advance. And many companies **simply won't allow their Serbian-plated cars into Kosovo** (and sometimes Albania) at all — partly an insurance issue, partly a safety one, given a history of Serbian-registered cars being targeted there. Tell the rental company exactly where you intend to drive, and get the permitted countries confirmed in writing.

## The Kosovo border rule worth knowing

One specific rule catches road-trippers out and is worth stating plainly: if your route includes both Serbia and Kosovo, **enter Serbia first, then go to Kosovo — not the other way around.** Serbia does not treat crossing from Serbia into Kosovo as leaving the country, so that direction is fine and you'll simply get a Kosovo entry stamp. But if you enter Kosovo first and then try to cross into Serbia, Serbian border control may treat it as an **illegal entry** and turn you back. On top of that, the **Green Card does not cover Kosovo**, so you'd buy separate insurance at the border (reported at around €15 for 15 days). Plan the order of countries around this, and confirm your rental even permits Kosovo before you set off.

## When renting is the right call

To bring it back to the practical decision: rent a car in Belgrade when your trip is built around **getting out of the city** — the Vojvodina day trips, the monasteries and wineries of Fruška Gora, the Iron Gates gorge on the Danube, or a multi-country Balkan loop. For the Belgrade portion of a trip, you'll move faster and spend less on foot, on the free trams, and in the odd cheap taxi. Match the rental to the days you're actually driving, and it goes from an expensive nuisance to one of the best ways to see the region.

## What's still unknown

Some specifics here are perishable and provider-dependent, so confirm them at the point of booking rather than trusting any single figure. Rental rates, deposit amounts and insurance excesses vary widely by company and season. Cross-border policies — especially around Kosovo and Albania — differ from one firm to the next and can change, so the only reliable source is the company you book with. And parking tariffs and fine amounts are periodically revised by the city. Treat the rules of thumb above as the durable shape of the decision, and pin down the exact numbers when you reserve.
