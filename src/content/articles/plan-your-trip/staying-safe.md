## Is Belgrade safe?

Yes — for ordinary visitors, Belgrade is a broadly safe city, and the most honest answer to "is Belgrade safe?" is a reassuring one with a few specific caveats. Official tourism and government portals describe Serbia as generally safe, with a low crime rate relative to many European destinations. Belgrade and other major cities are characterised as very safe, with no "no-go zones," and Western government advisories — while cautious — identify no systemic risk to ordinary tourists beyond standard urban concerns. As of mid-2026 there is no current advisory change that alters this picture, though advisory levels are perishable and worth a fresh check close to your travel dates.

The more detailed picture is reassuring too. Violent crime exists mainly in organised-crime contexts and around high-profile sporting events, and is not typically aimed at tourists. What you should plan around is ordinary petty theft: pickpocketing and bag-snatching happen in crowded areas, on public transport, at markets, and at motorway service stations. Treat your phone and wallet the way you would in any busy European capital, and you have covered most of the real risk.

## Is Belgrade safe for solo female travellers?

Largely, yes. Belgrade is widely regarded as one of the safer Eastern European capitals for women travelling alone, and most solo female travellers report feeling comfortable, including walking in busy central areas after dark. Reported accounts (travel-safety sites and solo-travel communities, so treat as indicative rather than official) note that unsolicited verbal attention is less common in Belgrade than in some other Balkan capitals.

The precautions are the universal big-city ones rather than anything Belgrade-specific. Keep an eye on your belongings in crowds and packed venues, use official or app-based taxis rather than unmarked cars (see the scams below), and avoid poorly lit or deserted areas late at night. Basing yourself in a central, walkable neighbourhood — Stari Grad, around Knez Mihailova and Republic Square, or [Skadarlija](/glossary/skadarlija) — keeps you close to busy streets and taxi ranks and reduces the need for long late-night walks alone.

## Is Belgrade safe at night?

Yes, with ordinary caution. Official tourism sources stress that Belgrade has no explicitly unsafe districts, and people move freely day and night; the central, pedestrianised core stays lively well into the evening. The sensible cautions are the universal ones: avoid poorly lit or deserted areas late at night, and for longer late journeys take a registered or app-based taxi rather than walking — particularly along the darker riverfront stretches between nightlife venues.

The one crowd-specific caution worth naming: high-profile football matches. Occasional hooligan-related trouble clusters around derby days and big fixtures, so give match-day crowds and stadium surroundings a wide berth if you're not attending — the trouble is between fan groups, not aimed at visitors, but there's no reason to be adjacent to it.

## The scams that actually exist

Belgrade's scam list is short and stable, and all of it is avoidable with a few habits.

**Airport and station taxi touts.** Aggressive unlicensed drivers approach arriving passengers in or near the airport terminal, offering rides at inflated or undisclosed prices — a problem the airport operator has itself acknowledged, and foreign offices warn that some drivers overcharge, especially at night or with foreigners. The fix is mechanical: use the airport's official taxi voucher system or a registered rank, and ignore every unsolicited offer inside or outside the terminal. The same rule applies at bus and train stations. The full arrival playbook is in [airport to city](/plan-your-trip/airport-to-city).

**Exchange-office and card tricks.** Some exchange offices — particularly in malls and near transport hubs — post poor rates or hide commissions, and ATMs and card terminals may nudge you toward "dynamic currency conversion" (paying in your home currency), which adds a markup. Decline DCC every time — always choose to pay in dinars — and change money at central exchange offices or banks. The detail lives in [money in Belgrade](/plan-your-trip/money).

**Pickpocketing in the predictable places.** Crowded public transport, markets, busy pedestrian streets and packed nightlife venues. Nothing exotic: front pockets, zipped bags, and phones off café table edges.

## Is Belgrade's nightlife safe?

Largely yes. Crowded clubs and river barges (splavovi) can see occasional fights or pickpocketing, but such incidents are not systemic — the sensible version is watchfulness, not avoidance. Keep an eye on your belongings in packed venues, agree or confirm prices (drinks, tables, minimum spends) before committing so the bill holds no surprises, and end the night with a registered or app-based taxi rather than a long dark walk along the river.

## Emergency numbers

Worth saving before you fly. **Police 192. Fire brigade 193. Ambulance 194.** The pan-European emergency number **112** also works in Serbia and is often the single number recommended to tourists. Most countries maintain consular sections at their Belgrade embassies for lost-passport and genuine-emergency situations — know your embassy's number if you like belt-and-braces planning.

## Is the tap water safe to drink?

Yes. Belgrade's tap water is treated and considered safe to drink, meeting national and EU drinking-water standards, and most locals drink it daily — there are public drinking fountains in the centre. It can carry a mild chlorine taste, so travellers with sensitive stomachs sometimes prefer filtered or bottled water for comfort rather than necessity. The caveat is geographic: tap water in rural Serbia outside the cities is less consistently reliable (as of 2026).

## Healthcare, pharmacies and insurance

The system in one paragraph: Serbia provides universal healthcare to citizens and residents, and **emergency care is generally available to foreigners** — but non-emergency doctor services usually require upfront cash payment, and while bilateral health agreements exist with some countries, many visitors are not covered. The practical conclusion is unambiguous: **comprehensive travel medical insurance is strongly recommended** for any Belgrade trip.

For the everyday end of health, Serbia's pharmacy network is dense — around 2,900 pharmacies nationwide (reported), with strong coverage in Belgrade. Pharmacies dispense both prescription and over-the-counter medicines, English is often spoken at central locations, and some operate 24 hours (specific hours and locations are perishable — check locally). For anything more than a pharmacy visit, your insurer's assistance line is the right first call.
