## Is Belgrade safe?

Yes — for ordinary visitors, Belgrade is a broadly safe city, and the most honest answer to "is Belgrade safe?" is a reassuring one with a few specific caveats. Official tourism and government portals describe Serbia as generally safe, with a low crime rate relative to many European destinations. Belgrade and other major cities are characterised as very safe, with no "no-go zones," and Western government advisories — while cautious — identify no systemic risk to ordinary tourists beyond standard urban concerns. As of mid-2026 there is no current advisory change that alters this picture, though advisory levels are perishable and worth a fresh check close to your travel dates.

The more detailed picture is reassuring too. Violent crime exists mainly in organised-crime contexts and around high-profile sporting events, and is not typically aimed at tourists. What you should plan around is ordinary petty theft: pickpocketing and bag-snatching happen in crowded areas, on public transport, at markets, and at motorway service stations. Treat your phone and wallet the way you would in any busy European capital, and you have covered most of the real risk.

## Is Belgrade safe for solo female travellers?

Largely, yes. Belgrade is widely regarded as one of the safer Eastern European capitals for women travelling alone, and most solo female travellers report feeling comfortable, including walking in busy central areas after dark. Reported accounts (travel-safety sites and solo-travel communities, so treat as indicative rather than official) note that unsolicited verbal attention is less common in Belgrade than in some other Balkan capitals.

The precautions are the universal big-city ones rather than anything Belgrade-specific. Keep an eye on your belongings in crowds and packed venues, use official or app-based taxis rather than unmarked cars (see the scams below), and avoid poorly lit or deserted areas late at night. Basing yourself in a central, walkable neighbourhood — Stari Grad, around Knez Mihailova and Republic Square, or Skadarlija — keeps you close to busy streets and taxi ranks and reduces the need for long late-night walks alone.

## Is Belgrade safe at night?

Yes, with ordinary caution. Official tourism sources stress that Belgrade has no explicitly unsafe districts, and people move freely day and night; the central, pedestrianised core stays lively well into the evening. The sensible cautions are the universal ones: avoid poorly lit or deserted areas late at night, and for longer late journeys take a registered or app-based taxi rather than walking far alone. One Belgrade-specific note: give a wide berth to crowds gathering around high-profile football matches, the main context in which occasional hooligan-related trouble occurs — it is not aimed at tourists, but it is easy to avoid.

## What scams should I watch for in Belgrade?

### Taxi and airport touts

Aggressive unlicensed taxi drivers approach arriving passengers in or near the airport terminal, offering rides at inflated or undisclosed prices; the airport operator has acknowledged the problem. Some drivers overcharge, especially at night or when dealing with foreigners. The fix is simple: use the airport's official voucher system and registered taxis or ranks, and ignore unsolicited offers. The same caution applies at bus and train stations. For the step-by-step on the voucher and fares, see our guide to [getting from the airport to the city](/plan-your-trip/airport-to-city).

### Exchange-rate and money tricks

Poor rates and hidden commissions turn up at some exchange offices, particularly in malls and near transport hubs. ATMs and card terminals may also nudge you toward dynamic currency conversion (DCC), which quietly adds a markup. Decline DCC when offered, and change money at central offices or banks rather than the first booth you see. We cover this in more depth on the [money in Belgrade](/plan-your-trip/money) page.

### Pickpocketing and where it happens

The everyday risk in Belgrade is petty theft rather than anything more serious. Pickpocketing and bag-snatching happen in the predictable places: crowded areas, on public transport, at markets, and at motorway service stations on the way in or out of the city. The mitigations are the same ones you would use in any busy European capital — keep your phone and wallet secure rather than in an open back pocket or a loose outer bag, stay aware in a press of people, and take particular care in packed venues. Do that and you have covered most of the real risk.

## Is Belgrade's nightlife safe?

Largely, yes. Official tourism sources stress that Belgrade has no explicitly unsafe districts. People move freely day and night, and the only sensible caution is the universal one: avoid poorly lit or deserted areas late at night. Nightlife spots — crowded clubs and the river barges known as *splavovi* — can see the occasional fight or pickpocketing, but these incidents are not systemic. Keep an eye on your belongings in a packed venue, and you are doing the main thing that matters. Which specific venues are currently "hot" shifts over time, so this is a pattern to plan around rather than a fixed list.

## Emergency numbers

These are the most useful facts on this page, so keep them handy:

- **Police:** 192
- **Fire brigade:** 193
- **Ambulance:** 194
- **Pan-European emergency number:** 112 (valid in Serbia and often recommended for tourists)

Foreign travellers' embassies maintain consular sections in Belgrade for emergencies, which is worth noting if you lose a passport or run into a more serious problem.

## Is the tap water safe to drink in Belgrade?

Yes. Belgrade's tap water is treated and considered safe to drink: it meets national and EU drinking-water standards, most locals drink it daily, and there are even public drinking fountains in the centre. It can carry a mild chlorine taste, and travellers with sensitive stomachs sometimes prefer filtered or bottled water simply for peace of mind. This applies to the city; as of 2026, tap water in rural parts of Serbia outside the major cities is less consistently reliable, so the in-Belgrade "drink the tap" advice doesn't automatically transfer to a remote village stop. Sparkling and still bottled water are widely sold everywhere if you would rather not.

## Health care and why insurance matters

Serbia provides universal healthcare to its citizens and residents. As a visitor, you can generally access emergency care, but non-emergency doctor services usually require upfront cash payment. Bilateral health agreements exist with some countries; many visitors, however, are not covered by them. Because of that gap, comprehensive travel medical insurance is strongly recommended — it is the single most practical step for protecting yourself against an unexpected bill.

For everyday needs, Serbia has a dense pharmacy network — reported as around 2,900 nationwide — with strong coverage in Belgrade. Pharmacies dispense both prescription and over-the-counter medicines, English is often spoken in central ones, and some operate 24 hours. Specific hours and locations change, so check locally rather than relying on a fixed list.

## What's still unknown

A few details on this topic are perishable and shouldn't be treated as settled. Western advisory levels can change, the specific venues that are currently "hot" shift over time, individual pharmacy hours vary, and the exact terms of bilateral health agreements differ by country and are not something to assume. Tap-water quality outside the major cities is uneven and not something to generalise from Belgrade. When any of these matters for your trip, confirm the current situation close to your travel dates rather than relying on a general guide.
