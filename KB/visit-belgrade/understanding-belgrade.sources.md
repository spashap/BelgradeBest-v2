## 1. The thesis — why Belgrade looks the way it does

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade sits at the confluence of the Sava and Danube rivers, a strategic crossing point that has been fortified for over two millennia and conquered, razed and rebuilt many times. Its cityscape is a layered collage rather than a single unified style — Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, neoclassical, socialist-modernist and contemporary fabric coexist, often on adjacent streets.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1; Balkan Insight "Serbia's History is Carved in Stone"; Architecture of Serbia overview)

[CONTEXT] This "layered, not pretty" character is the visitor's interpretive key: most of the city's individual layers were partly erased by the next ruler, so understanding Belgrade means reading the surviving fragments of each era rather than expecting a preserved old town in one style.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §A.3, §C.1)

## 2. The layers, in order

[CONFIRMED] **Celtic / Roman (Singidunum).** The fortress site holds over 2,000 years of continuous fortification, beginning with a Roman castrum in the 1st–2nd century AD. Most Roman fabric is now underground; surface-visible Roman elements are fragmentary (e.g. the shaft underlying the later Roman Well, occasional masonry). For a visitor the layer is largely conceptual — best grasped via the fortress grounds and the National Museum's archaeological collections.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1; SrpskiKod Kalemegdan article, 2019)

[CONFIRMED] **Byzantine / medieval Serbian.** The fortress was rebuilt as a Byzantine castle in the 12th century and became the seat of Serbian Despot Stefan Lazarević in the early 15th century, when Belgrade was capital of the Serbian Despotate. Surviving elements: the remains of the Despot's castle and donjon on the Upper Town panorama plateau, and Nebojša Tower in the Lower Town (a ticketed museum site).
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1; SrpskiKod Kalemegdan article)

[REPORTED] **Ottoman period.** Belgrade fell to the Ottomans in 1521 and was under Ottoman control for much of the following centuries, with intermittent Austrian occupations. The "roughly 500 years of Ottoman rule" often cited in popular narratives is a simplification — control was fragmented. Under the Ottomans the fortress plateau served as a military parade ground (the Turkish name *fićir-bajir*, "hill for contemplation").
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1; SrpskiKod; Belgrade Beat)

[CONFIRMED] Ottoman traces that survive: stone ramparts in the Upper Town (the later brick facing is 18th-century Austrian work), a pasha's tomb and older gates inside Kalemegdan, and the Bajrakli Mosque — the only survivor of the 200+ mosques that once stood in the city.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1; walking-tour route sources)

[CONFIRMED] **Austrian / Habsburg.** The fortress was repeatedly upgraded by Austrian forces during their occupations (notably 1717–1739), who rebuilt parts of the artillery fortifications. Across the Danube, Zemun was under Habsburg rule 1717–1918 and developed as a separate frontier town — the most legible Austro-Hungarian layer in greater Belgrade. (Zemun has its own page; cross-listed below.)
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1)

[CONFIRMED] **Interwar Yugoslavia.** After WWI, Belgrade became capital of the new South-Slav kingdom; the Republic Square area carries the pre-WWII civic fabric (National Museum building, National Theatre). Zemun was incorporated as a municipality in 1934.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1)

[CONFIRMED] **Socialist Yugoslavia — modernism & Brutalism.** New Belgrade (Novi Beograd) was planned and built after WWII as a monumental socialist city on the left bank of the Sava (municipality 1952; block construction from 1948). It is the city's signature modernist/Brutalist layer. (Detailed building inventory lives on the `brutalist-belgrade` page; cross-listed below.)
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; Greyscape "Brutalist Belgrade")

[CONFIRMED] **Post-2000 — Belgrade Waterfront.** Beograd na vodi, on the right bank of the Sava, began with a 2014 agreement between the Serbian government and Abu Dhabi–based Eagle Hills; it adds glass-and-steel towers (including the 168 m Kula Belgrade), a large mall and promenades, in deliberate contrast to socialist New Belgrade across the river. Early towers and the mall were occupied by 2022–24.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1; Belgrade Waterfront overview)

[REPORTED] The Waterfront is framed in critical writing as investor-driven urbanism and is debated locally; present it as the current layer visitors physically see on the Sava riverfront, noting the debate rather than taking a side.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.1; Failed Architecture / Eurozine critical essays, 2015)

## 3. Belgrade Fortress (Kalemegdan) — the place where all layers are visible

[CONFIRMED] Kalemegdan Park and Belgrade Fortress occupy the promontory above the Sava–Danube confluence and contain layered remains of Roman, Byzantine, medieval Serbian, Ottoman and Austrian fortifications. The park and fortress grounds are open 24/7 year-round and free to enter; it offers the city's best panoramic view over the confluence.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.3, §E.1; Trip.com Kalemegdan listing, 2026-06; official Belgrade Fortress visitor info)

[CONFIRMED] The fortress's individual interior attractions are separately ticketed and seasonal: Roman Well, Military Bunker, Big Powder Magazine, Casemates/Military Museum, Nebojša Tower and the Clock Tower (Sahat kula). As of 2026 the seasonal pattern is 15 April–15 October 11:00–19:00 (last entry 18:30) and 15 October–15 April 10:00–17:00 (last entry 16:30), Tuesday–Sunday (Nebojša Tower Wednesday–Sunday). Hours are perishable — state the seasonal mechanism, mark times indicative, "as of 2026."
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §E.1; official beogradskatvrdjava.co.rs visitor info, accessed 2026-06-06)

[CONFIRMED] Per-site ticket prices as of 2026 (single / reduced, RSD): Clock Tower 80 / 40; Roman Well 120 / 60; Nebojša Tower 200 / 100; Big Powder Magazine 200 / 100. Indicative/perishable.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §E.1; official beogradskatvrdjava.co.rs)

[UNKNOWN] The Military Bunker follows the same seasonal hours, but its exact 2026 single-ticket price was not visible in the official source captured — do not state a Bunker price as settled.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §E.1)

[CONFLICT] A combined/unified fortress ticket (covering ~4–5 interior sites) was announced in 2017–18 at roughly 400–450 RSD, but more recent ticketing pages emphasise single-site tickets and online purchase via ticket.rs without clearly repeating the unified ticket. Whether a combined ticket is available in 2026 is therefore not confirmed: present it as "a combined ticket has existed in the past; check availability at the fortress info/souvenir desk or ticket.rs on arrival," not as a current guaranteed product.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §E.1; FeelBelgrade 2017; tourism press 2018; belgradecard.rs)

## 4. How to experience the layers (visitor framing)

[REPORTED] The standard free walking-tour spine — Republic Square (Prince Mihailo monument) → Knez Mihailova → Kalemegdan → Skadarlija, ~2–2.5 hours, tip-based (commonly €10–20) — is the most efficient way for a first-timer to have the layers narrated in one coherent walk. Thematic variants exist (20th-century/NATO-bombing history; "Mysterious"/underground including the Roman well and bunkers). Tour prices and schedules are perishable.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §A.4, §E.6; GuruWalk, Belgrade Free Tour, Belgrade Walking Tours, Belgrade Beat, 2026)
