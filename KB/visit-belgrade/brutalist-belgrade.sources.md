## 1. Why Belgrade is a Brutalism destination (at a glance)

[CONFIRMED] New Belgrade (Novi Beograd), on the left bank of the Sava, was planned and built after WWII as a monumental socialist city — designated a municipality in 1952, with residential "Blok" construction from 1948. It functions today as an open-air catalogue of socialist modernist and Brutalist planning and housing.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; Greyscape "Brutalist Belgrade")

[CONTEXT] Belgrade's Brutalism is under-served in mainstream travel content (OTAs don't cover it; existing material is scattered single-author blogs), which is exactly why a structured visitor guide has room to be authoritative. Most sites are living residential neighbourhoods, not ticketed attractions — the visitor experience is walking/observing, not entering.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §A.3, §C.2)

## 2. The landmark buildings

[CONFIRMED] **Genex Tower / Western City Gate.** A 35-floor twin-tower complex in New Belgrade, designed by Mihajlo Mitrović, built 1977–80, with a now-closed rotating restaurant at the top. The residential tower remains inhabited; the commercial tower is largely empty and inaccessible. Best experienced from outside / surrounding streets.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; Serbia.com "Genex Tower," 2025)

[CONFIRMED] **Eastern City Gate (Rudo).** Three 28-storey residential towers completed 1973–76, brutalist triangular/step-like form, ~190 apartments each (~1,500 residents by the late 1980s). Fully residential and in use; the area is publicly accessible but interiors are private.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; SOSBrutalism entry)

[CONFIRMED] **Karaburma "Toblerone" Tower.** A 17-storey residential tower completed 1963, designed by Rista Šekerinski, nicknamed "Toblerone" for its jagged triangular balconies. Inhabited residential building — view from surrounding streets only.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; SOSBrutalism / Wikipedia)

[REPORTED] **The New Belgrade Blocks (Blokovi).** Block 23 is repeatedly cited as one of the most "authentic" examples of the original masterplan; Blocks 61–63 and 28 recur in brutalism guides and photography threads. These are living residential areas with courtyards, schools and local services — visitors should treat them as neighbourhoods and stay on public paths; there is no official visitor policy or ticketing.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; Greyscape; MyWanderlust brutalist guide, 2025)

## 3. The visitable modernist venues

[CONFIRMED] **Museum of Contemporary Art (MoCAB / MSUB)** at Ušće (Block 15) is a flagship modernist museum building, reopened in 2017. As of 2026: Wednesday/Friday/Sunday 11:00–19:00, Thursday/Saturday 12:00–20:00, Monday 11:00–19:00, closed Tuesdays; ticket 600 RSD (300 reduced); free admission the first Wednesday of each month. Hours/prices perishable — indicative "as of 2026." (Full museum detail on `belgrade-museums`.)
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §E.2; official msub.org.rs, 2025-07-15)

[CONFIRMED] **Sava Centre**, a major late-Yugoslav congress and cultural centre, underwent reconstruction from 2021; the business/congress segment reopened 14 November 2023 and the reconstructed Blue Hall (capacity raised to ~4,050) reopened 3 September 2024. Visitable when hosting events.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; 2023–24 press coverage)

[CONFIRMED] **Palata Srbija (Palace of Serbia)**, a monumental former federal executive building (1961) in New Belgrade, is not generally open daily; access happens occasionally via special events such as European Heritage Days and curated architecture walks.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2; Belgrade tourism / architecture-festival announcements, 2024)

[REPORTED] Palata Srbija's special-access pattern is not a fixed schedule and may change year to year — present it as "occasionally open via heritage events; check current programmes," not a reliable walk-in.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2)

## 4. Seeing it as a route

[REPORTED] A half-day is the commonly suggested frame for a New Belgrade Brutalism walk/ride, combining selected blocks, Genex from the outside and MoCAB, optionally with Sava Centre and riverfront. Some operators run dedicated brutalism tours. Itinerary shapes and tour prices are perishable.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.2, §C.4; brutalism guides 2019–2026)

## 5. Key labels to honor when writing

- **Building histories** (architect, year, form) are CONFIRMED — state plainly.
- **Public-access status is the load-bearing honesty point:** Genex, Eastern City Gate, Karaburma and the Blocks are RESIDENTIAL — view from outside, stay on public paths, no entering, no ticketing. Never imply they're attractions to tour inside.
- **MoCAB hours/prices** are CONFIRMED-as-of-2026 but perishable — indicative, time-stamped; full detail belongs on `belgrade-museums` (link, don't duplicate).
- **Palata Srbija** is occasional-access only (REPORTED pattern) — never present as a reliable walk-in.
- **Sava Centre** is event-dependent.
