## 1. Why neighbourhoods matter in Belgrade (at a glance)

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade is a large city whose districts read almost like separate cities — Dorćol, Vračar, Zemun, New Belgrade and others each have a distinct personality and price point, and locals use district names constantly for orientation. Understanding the districts is the most useful first-timer framework.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4; "Why Everyone Is Suddenly Talking About Belgrade"; LOTUS EATERS TRAVEL)

[CONTEXT] This page is for visitors who care about *understanding the city's character* rather than nightlife or hotel listings (those live on other legs). It orients; the deep pages explain.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §A.3)

## 2. The districts

[CONFIRMED] **Stari Grad (Old Town).** The central historic municipality — Republic Square, Knez Mihailova, Kalemegdan, and parts of Dorćol and Skadarlija — mixing Habsburg-era, interwar and socialist buildings in a dense, walkable grid. The natural base for first-timers and the main node for free walking tours.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4)

[CONFIRMED] **Dorćol.** One of the most eclectic central districts, with an "upper" (more upscale) and "lower" (creative, up-and-coming) part, close to the Danube. Visitor use: café/bar/small-gallery hunting and the Danube promenade.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4; 2026 "Where to stay" guide)

[REPORTED] **Skadarlija.** A short cobblestone street within the Dorćol/Stari Grad area, framed as Belgrade's bohemian quarter (kafanas, live music), and described in some 2025 articles as the city's second-most-visited spot after Kalemegdan. The "second-most-visited" claim is likely marketing — present the bohemian character as real, the ranking as a claim.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4)

[REPORTED] **Savamala.** A historic warehouse/industrial district on the Sava, partly redeveloped; known for street art, creative spaces and clubs, and increasingly folded into "Belgrade Waterfront" narratives. Visitor use: walking between old Savamala streets, riverside barges and the new Waterfront promenades to read the city's transformation.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4; street-art guides 2019–2023)

[CONFIRMED] **Vračar.** A central district anchored by the Temple of St. Sava and the National Library, with cafés and quieter residential streets; the Nikola Tesla Museum is also here. Visitor use: combine St. Sava, Tesla Museum and cafés into a half-day.
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4; 2026 Expedia neighbourhood guide)

[CONFIRMED] **Zemun.** A former Austro-Hungarian frontier town, now a Belgrade municipality, preserving 18th–19th-century streets, a riverside quay and Gardoš hill; described as an "old soul," distinct from central Belgrade. (Full detail on the `zemun` page.)
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4)

[CONFIRMED] **New Belgrade (Novi Beograd).** The planned socialist city of large residential blocks, wide boulevards, offices and big venues (Genex, Sava Centre, Palata Srbija) — an open-air museum of socialist modernism. (Full detail on the `brutalist-belgrade` page.)
(Source: RESEARCH_visit-belgrade.md §C.4; Greyscape)
