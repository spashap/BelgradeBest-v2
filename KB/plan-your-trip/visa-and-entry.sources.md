## 1. Visa-free entry and the 90/180 rule

[CONFIRMED] Citizens of the EU, UK, USA, Canada, Australia and many other countries can visit Serbia visa-free for up to 90 days within a 180-day period, for tourism or business. The "90 days in 180" is counted on a rolling basis similar to the Schengen model (the 180-day window starts from first entry).
(Source: deep research §3.1, citing MFA pages and embassy advisories)

[CONFIRMED] The exact list of visa-free nationalities can change (occasional suspensions for particular countries have happened). Visitors must verify their own nationality and visa type with the Serbian Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA) before travelling.
(Source: deep research §3.1; MFA visa-requirements pages)

[REPORTED] For nationals who do require a visa, the short-stay Visa C covers up to 90 days within 180 days for tourism, business, family visits, events or conferences.
(Source: deep research §3.3)

## 2. The valid-Schengen/UK/US/EU-visa exemption

[CONFIRMED] Under a long-standing Serbian government decision (30 October 2014), foreigners who would otherwise need a Serbian visa may enter Serbia without one if they hold a valid visa or residence permit from Schengen, the UK, an EU member state or the USA. They may stay up to 90 days within a 180-day period, only while that underlying visa/residence remains valid; if it is in an expired passport, they can carry both old and new passports together.
(Source: deep research §3.2)

[CONFIRMED] This exemption is a legal structure that is stable, but travellers should confirm their specific case, as conditions and suspensions can apply.
(Source: deep research §3.2)

## 3. e-Visa, ETIAS and ETA — what does NOT apply

[CONFIRMED] Serbia is not in the Schengen Area: the EU's ETIAS scheme does not apply to entering Serbia, and the UK ETA does not apply to Serbia. For most short-stay tourists there is no Serbia-specific online pre-registration as of 2026; those who need a visa generally apply via embassies/consulates.
(Source: deep research §3.3)

[REPORTED] Serbia has moved some functions onto an online portal for foreigners, but mainstream short-stay tourists still typically use embassy/consulate channels where a visa is required. Perishable — any move to an e-Visa would be widely reported.
(Source: deep research §3.3)

## 4. The 24-hour police registration (white card / Bela Karta) — the thing visitors miss

[CONFIRMED] Every foreigner must have their stay registered with the local police within 24 hours of arrival, regardless of the reason for the stay. Accommodation providers (hotels, hostels, and individuals renting for compensation) and people hosting a foreign visitor are legally required to register the visitor within 24 hours; the foreign national is also required to register their stay and any change of address within 24 hours.
(Source: Serbian MFA "Registration of Stay" page; deep research §3.4)

[CONFIRMED] In practice: hotels, hostels and managed/registered apartments do this automatically at check-in — guests usually need not visit the police in person and receive a copy of the white card. If staying in a private apartment, with friends/family, or an unregistered rental, the host must register the guest at the local police station within 24 hours — or the guest must do it themselves. Some Airbnb hosts do not handle this, which can leave guests technically unregistered.
(Source: deep research §3.4; relocation/embassy guidance, 2026)

[REPORTED] The white card should be carried with the passport during the stay; it may be collected on departure, and is needed if later applying for residence. Failing to register can lead to fines or administrative problems at departure or in future applications. Enforcement detail (paper vs digital card, how strictly checked) is perishable.
(Source: deep research §3.4)

[CONFIRMED] If you change address during your stay, you must update the registration at the police station for the new address within 24 hours.
(Source: MFA "Registration of Stay"; deep research §3.4)

## 5. Where short-stay ends (signpost to Invest & Relocate)

[CONFIRMED] Tourist/short business visits are up to 90 days within 180 days. Longer stays, employment and many forms of study require temporary or permanent residence permits and the appropriate entry visa (e.g. Visa D). Permanent residents have a different registration regime (eight days to register domicile).
(Source: deep research §3.5; MFA pages)

[CONTEXT] Detailed residence, work and digital-nomad pathways are out of scope for this arrival-focused page and are covered in the Invest & Relocate leg. Provide only a one-line signpost + cross-link here.
(Source: deep research §3.5, §10.3)
