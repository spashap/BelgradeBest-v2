## 1. Currency and the payment landscape

[CONFIRMED] Serbia's currency is the Serbian dinar (RSD). The euro is not legal tender, though many businesses quote prices in EUR as a reference. Credit and debit cards are widely accepted in cities and tourist businesses, but cash is still preferred in smaller shops, kiosks, green markets, many taxis and rural areas. (Structural facts — stable.)
(Source: deep research §4.1)

---

## 2. ATMs, fees and dynamic currency conversion (DCC)

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade has dense ATM coverage through local and international banks.
(Source: deep research §4.2)

[CONFIRMED] Dynamic Currency Conversion (DCC) — when an ATM or card terminal offers to charge in your home currency instead of dinars — typically carries a markup over the card network's rate. Card networks themselves advise that DCC involves additional fees and that travellers can decline it. Best practice: withdraw and pay in local currency (RSD) and decline DCC, letting your own bank handle conversion. (DCC as a mechanism is stable; exact fee levels and bank surcharges are perishable.)
(Source: deep research §4.2, citing card-network guidance)

## 3. Exchange offices and common rip-offs

[REPORTED] Specialist exchange offices and bank branches in the city centre generally offer good rates with minimal commission. Exchange kiosks inside the airport, bus/train stations and shopping malls often have poorer rates or extra commissions — best avoided except for small sums. (The "centre good, station/mall worse" spread is a stable pattern; exact spreads and best chains are perishable.)
(Source: deep research §4.3)

## 4. Tipping norms

[REPORTED] Tipping norms (broadly agreed across guides; soft-stable):
 – Restaurants and cafés: optional but appreciated; around 10–15% or rounding up where service is good.
 – Fast food, bakeries, coffee-to-go: no tip expected.
 – Taxis: not required; rounding up is common.
 – Bars and hotels: ~10% or small fixed tips for porters/housekeeping if desired.
The cultural expectation (appreciated, not mandatory, ~10%) is stable; exact percentages are not fixed.
(Source: deep research §4.4)

## 5. Rough daily costs

[REPORTED] There is no single authoritative current-price index in the sources. In general, Belgrade is relatively affordable compared with Western Europe, helped by free public transport and reasonably priced food; budget travellers can manage on a modest daily spend. Accommodation and dining prices are rising ahead of Expo-related demand. Any specific price figures must be labelled perishable and given as ranges, not fixed amounts.
(Source: deep research §4.5)
