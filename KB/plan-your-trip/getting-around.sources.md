## 1. The headline: public transport is free

[CONFIRMED] From 1 January 2025, Belgrade introduced completely free public transport on all city and suburban lines — buses, trams, trolleybuses and BG Voz suburban trains. No tickets, passes or validation are required for residents or visitors. (Belgrade became the largest European city by population to adopt universal fare-free transit.)
(Source: Tourist Organisation of Belgrade (tob.rs), 2025; deep research §2.1, multiple 2025–26 sources)

[REPORTED] The system is funded from the city budget and higher local taxes rather than fares; the policy has drawn some domestic criticism but was still in force in mid-2026. It is perishable in the medium term (a future administration could change it) but stable for visitors in 2026 and likely 2027 unless a major change is announced.
(Source: deep research §2.1)

## 2. Historical ticketing context (why old guides mention cards)

[CONTEXT] Belgrade previously used a smart-card fare system — BusPlus (2012 until its contract ended April 2023), replaced by Beograd Plus from May 2023 (payment via SMS, app, and limited physical outlets). Once transport became free in 2025, ticket-purchase functions were switched off; the Beograd Plus app now serves journey info, route search and service updates rather than payment. Included only to explain why older blogs talk about buying BusPlus cards or SMS tickets.
(Source: deep research §2.2)

## 3. What a visitor actually needs to know

[CONFIRMED] You can board any city bus, tram, trolleybus or BG Voz train within the Belgrade system without buying a ticket, as fares are currently free. Services are dense within the urban core, with multiple lines through hubs such as Zeleni Venac, Slavija, the Republic Square area and the New Belgrade blocks.
(Source: deep research §2.3)

[REPORTED] For route planning and live departures, visitors can use the Beograd Plus app, Moovit, or Google Maps. The existence of specific lines (e.g. line 36 linking Prokop to Slavija, BG Voz core routes) is stable; timetables and headways are perishable.
(Source: deep research §2.3)

## 4. Walkability of the centre

[CONFIRMED] Central Belgrade (Stari Grad and parts of Vračar/Savski Venac) is reasonably walkable but hilly in places. Knez Mihailova, Republic Square, Kalemegdan Fortress and the Sava riverfront (Belgrade Waterfront) form a contiguous pedestrian-friendly area with wide pavements and significant car-free zones. Slopes are noticeable walking from the river up toward Slavija or St. Sava Temple; those with luggage or limited mobility may prefer a short tram/bus or taxi for uphill segments. (Physical characteristics are stable.)
(Source: deep research §2.4)

## 5. Taxis vs ride-hail inside the city

[CONFIRMED] Numerous licensed taxi associations operate; official taxis must display a municipal registration number and taxi sign. Foreign-office guidance recommends using registered taxis ordered by phone or association app rather than hailing random cars, to avoid overcharging.
(Source: deep research §2.5)

[REPORTED] Daytime tariffs are city-regulated; early-2025 figures cited a starting fare around 320 RSD and ~105 RSD/km — indicative and perishable. App-based options Yandex Go and CarGo offer upfront pricing and card payment; CarGo's regulatory status is contested but it continues to operate. Best practice (use apps or phone-ordered cabs; avoid unmarked vehicles and touts) is stable; tariffs and active apps are perishable.
(Source: deep research §2.5)

## 6. Cycling and bike-share

[REPORTED] Belgrade has expanding but incomplete cycling infrastructure (~100 km of lanes reported in the early 2020s, with more planned). A public bike-share system has been tendered (figures range from 150 docks/900 bikes to ~1,000 bikes/150 stations), concentrated in New Belgrade and central municipalities; deployment status and tourist access (apps, pricing) are perishable. Several private rental operators (e.g. iBikeBelgrade, Belgrade Bike Central) offer city and e-bikes hourly or daily from central locations. (Private rentals and general infrastructure level are stable; municipal bike-share specifics are perishable.)
(Source: deep research §2.6)

## 7. Accessibility (brief, honest)

[REPORTED] Accessibility is uneven and system-wide data is limited. Free transport helps, but many older trams, buses and pavements remain challenging for wheelchair users; newer venues and the Expo site are designed with accessibility in mind. Present honestly as mixed, not solved.
(Source: deep research §2.5/§8.5)
