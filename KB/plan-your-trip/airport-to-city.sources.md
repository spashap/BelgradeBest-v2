## 1. The airport and the options at a glance

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade's airport is Nikola Tesla Airport (BEG), about 18 km from the city centre; typical road journey times are 20–40 minutes depending on traffic.
(Source: Belgrade practical-arrival deep research, 2026, §1.1, citing airport/aviation sources)

[CONFIRMED] The current ways from the airport into Belgrade are: regular city buses (lines 72, 600, 607 — free since January 2025), the paid A1 shuttle minibus to Slavija, the official fixed-price airport taxi voucher system, and app-based rides (Yandex Go, CarGo). A future airport–city railway is under construction but not in service as of mid-2026.
(Source: deep research §1.1)

## 2. Free public buses (72, 600, 607)

[CONFIRMED] As of 1 January 2025, all regular Belgrade city public transport became free for all passengers — no tickets, fares or validation on regular city lines. This makes the airport buses free to ride. (Policy current as of 2026; a fare policy is perishable and could change — re-check closer to travel.)
(Source: Tourist Organisation of Belgrade (tob.rs) "City public transport" page, 2025; deep research §1.2, §2.1)

[CONFIRMED] Bus 72 runs Airport → New Belgrade → Zeleni Venac (a central downtown terminus just below Knez Mihailova and Republic Square). The stop is directly outside the terminal. A night variant 72N serves Republic Square. Travel time is typically 35–50 minutes depending on traffic.
(Source: deep research §1.2)

[CONFIRMED] Bus 600 runs Airport → New Belgrade → Beograd Centar ("Prokop") main railway station — the best option for passengers connecting directly to trains. Travel time approximately 30–45 minutes.
(Source: deep research §1.3)

[REPORTED] A third regular line, 607, serves the airport and parts of New Belgrade and Banovo Brdo — useful mainly for travellers staying in western riverside neighbourhoods rather than the old centre. Also free as part of the regular network.
(Source: deep research §1.4)

[REPORTED] Older guides and videos still quote small cash bus fares (e.g. 45–150 RSD) — these predate the 2025 free-transport policy and are now outdated. Do not present any per-ticket bus fare as current.
(Source: deep research §1.2, noting the contradiction between old fare sources and current free policy)

## 3. The A1 shuttle (paid exception)

[CONFIRMED] The A1 shuttle minibus runs Airport → Fontana (New Belgrade) → main bus/rail area → Slavija Square, with limited stops, making it faster into the centre than bus 72. Slavija is a transport hub walkable to Vračar, the St. Sava Temple area and many hotels. A1 is a separate paid service — the exception to free regular transport.
(Source: deep research §1.5)

[REPORTED] The A1 fare is commonly quoted at about 400 RSD (a little under 4 EUR), paid in cash on board; travel time around 30–40 minutes. Some earlier sources quote lower fares (200 RSD, 2–2.5 EUR), indicating a gradual increase — treat as indicative ("low single-digit euros, paid in cash; check current fare"), not fixed. Perishable.
(Source: deep research §1.5)

## 4. Official airport taxi (voucher system)

[CONFIRMED] Nikola Tesla Airport operates an official fixed-price taxi system to prevent overcharging: passengers obtain a voucher from airport e-kiosks or the TAXI INFO counter in arrivals showing a fixed fare by destination zone; the driver must charge only that amount. After getting the voucher, proceed to the first official taxi in the queue in front of the terminal; queue-skipping is not permitted. Vouchers cover up to four passengers and normal luggage.
(Source: deep research §1.6, citing the airport "Taxi service" page)

[REPORTED] Airport-to-city-centre by voucher is most commonly quoted around 3,000–3,500 RSD (roughly 25–30 EUR at recent rates); non-official zone tables suggest a wider 20–90 EUR range by zone. Official airport pages give the mechanism, not specific figures. Indicative/perishable.
(Source: deep research §1.6)

[REPORTED] Aggressive unlicensed taxi touts operate in the arrivals hall despite the voucher system; the airport operator has acknowledged this. Use only the official TAXI INFO desk and marked taxi rank; ignore unsolicited offers. (Risk category is stable; specific tactics perishable.)
(Source: deep research §1.6, §6.2, citing airport operator VINCI and traveller reports)

## 5. Ride-hail apps

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade has no Uber or Bolt. The main app-based options are Yandex Go (Yango) — operating in Serbia with upfront fare estimates, card or cash — and CarGo, a local Uber-like platform. Classic licensed-taxi dispatch apps (e.g. Pink Taxi, Belgrade Taxi) also exist.
(Source: deep research §1.7)

[REPORTED] CarGo's regulatory status has been contested over the years, but it continues to operate in practice and is popular for car quality and transparent pricing. Which apps operate and under what legal framework is perishable; the broad pattern (no Uber/Bolt, Yandex Go + a local app present) is stable in the short term.
(Source: deep research §1.7)

## 6. The future airport–city railway

[CONFIRMED] A new ~18.3 km dual-track electrified passenger rail line is under construction, branching from the Belgrade–Novi Sad corridor at Zemun Polje and connecting Nikola Tesla Airport and the National Stadium / Expo site at Surčin. The airport station will be about 500 m from Terminal 1.
(Source: deep research §1.8)

[REPORTED] Nine new EMU trains for the BG Voz line were ordered from CRRC, with government sources targeting delivery by 31 December 2026; 2026 updates indicate construction on schedule for an operational launch in spring 2027, with an airport–centre journey of about 15–20 minutes. Treat the dates as provisional, not settled.
(Source: deep research §1.8)

[UNKNOWN] The ticketing regime for the rail link is not yet specified — whether rides will be free under the city's free-transport policy or carry a special airport surcharge has not been announced.
(Source: deep research §1.8)
