## 1. General safety picture

[CONFIRMED] Official tourism and government portals describe Serbia as generally safe with a low crime rate relative to many European destinations; Belgrade and other major cities are characterised as very safe with no "no-go zones." Western government advisories are cautious but identify no systemic risk to ordinary tourists beyond standard urban concerns.
(Source: deep research §6.1, citing official portals and advisories)

[REPORTED] Violent crime exists mainly in organised-crime contexts and around high-profile sporting events, not typically targeting tourists. Petty theft, pickpocketing and bag-snatching occur in crowded areas, on public transport, at markets and at motorway service stations. (Qualitative picture stable; advisory levels perishable.)
(Source: deep research §6.1)

## 2. Taxi and airport scams

[REPORTED] Aggressive unlicensed taxi drivers approach arriving passengers in or near the airport terminal offering rides at inflated or undisclosed prices; the airport operator has acknowledged the problem. Foreign offices warn some drivers overcharge, especially at night or with foreigners. Use the airport's official voucher system and registered taxis/ranks; ignore unsolicited offers; the same caution applies at bus and train stations. (Existence of these scams is stable; tactics and going rates perishable.)
(Source: deep research §6.2; cross-ref airport-to-city §4)

## 3. Exchange-rate and money tricks

[REPORTED] Poor rates and hidden commissions occur at some exchange offices, especially in malls and near transport hubs; ATMs and terminals may nudge toward DCC, which adds a markup. Decline DCC and exchange at central offices/banks. (Stable as risk categories; specifics perishable. Full money detail lives on the money page.)
(Source: deep research §6.3; cross-ref money §2–3)

## 4. Nightlife and area safety

[CONFIRMED] Official tourism sources stress Belgrade has no explicitly unsafe districts; people move freely day and night, with standard caution (avoid poorly lit or deserted areas late at night) sensible. Nightlife areas — crowded clubs and river barges (splavovi) — can see occasional fights or pickpocketing, but such incidents are not systemic. (Pattern stable; which specific venues are "hot" is perishable.)
(Source: deep research §6.4)

## 5. Emergency numbers (stable)

[CONFIRMED] Key emergency numbers in Serbia: Police 192; Fire brigade 193; Ambulance 194; pan-European emergency number 112 (valid in Serbia and often recommended for tourists). Foreign travellers' embassies maintain consular sections in Belgrade for emergencies.
(Source: deep research §6.5)

## 6. Health care access and insurance

[CONFIRMED] Serbia provides universal healthcare to citizens and residents; emergency care is generally available to foreigners, but non-emergency doctor services usually require upfront cash payment. Bilateral health agreements exist with some countries, but many visitors are not covered — comprehensive travel medical insurance is strongly recommended.
(Source: deep research §6.6)

[REPORTED] Serbia has a dense pharmacy network (around 2,900 nationwide), with strong coverage in Belgrade; pharmacies dispense prescription and over-the-counter medicines, English is often spoken in central pharmacies, and some operate 24 hours. (Structure stable; specific hours/locations perishable.)
(Source: deep research §8.6)
