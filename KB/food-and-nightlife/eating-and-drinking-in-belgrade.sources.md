## 1. Cash vs card

[CONFIRMED] Cards are widely accepted across urban Belgrade — most mid-range and high-end restaurants, splavovi and hotel bars take cards. Cash still matters at small establishments, markets, bakeries, kiosks and some older venues.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.1)

[REPORTED] Travellers commonly report being able to pay roughly 70–90% of city expenses by card, but are advised to always carry some dinars for the cash-leaning spots (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.1)

[CONFIRMED] Practical rule: expect card at mid/high-end restaurants, splavovi and hotel bars; carry cash for kafanas (especially tips), bakeries, markets, dessert shops and small local bars.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.1)

---

## 2. Tipping

[CONFIRMED] There is no legally enforced tipping norm in Serbia; tipping is discretionary appreciation for good service.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.2)

[REPORTED] The Belgrade consensus is to round up small café/restaurant bills and leave around 5–10% on full meals or larger groups when service is good; tipping is usually done in cash even when paying the bill by card (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.2)

## 3. Price benchmarks (perishable — indicative only)

[REPORTED] Indicative 2024–2026 benchmarks, all fluctuating with inflation and exchange rate — present each as reported and time-stamped, and tell readers to check current menus:
- Coffee: commonly reported around 150–300 RSD in non-touristy cafés, higher in prime spots.
- Club/splav drinks: beer roughly 2–4 EUR, cocktails roughly 5–7 EUR.
- A splav table example (2024): beer from ~590 RSD, cocktails from ~890 RSD, bottle table minimums from ~16,900 RSD; entry advertised free but tables carry minimum spend.
- A full kafana meal for two: reported around 4,000–8,000 RSD at a mid-range central/Skadarlija venue.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.4, §9.3)

## 4. Opening hours and the going-out rhythm

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade runs late: many restaurants serve until around 23:00 or later, while bars and clubs commonly stay open until roughly 04:00–06:00.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.4)

[REPORTED] The big going-out nights are typically described as Thursday through Sunday, with some venues running until ~08:00, and waterfront venues blending dining and nightlife with long daily hours (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.4)

## 5. Reservations and dress codes

[CONFIRMED] Reservations are effectively required for large-capacity splavovi and exclusive clubs, especially on weekends; fine-dining and Michelin-listed restaurants also strongly recommend booking for prime seating.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.5)

[REPORTED] Dress code: fine dining is smart-casual to elegant (shorts/sportswear frowned upon); splavovi and clubs are smart-casual minimum, with many banning sandals for men and enforcing an "elegant or smart-casual" door policy. Specific venue policies are perishable.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.5)

## 6. Drinking age and ID

[CONFIRMED] Serbia's legal drinking age is 18; the law targets sales to minors. ID checks are more common at busy nightclubs than at sit-down venues, and younger-looking guests are more likely to be carded — carry photo ID.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.6)

## 7. Late-night and 24-hour food

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade has a strong late-night food culture centred on burek, pizza, gyros and grilled meat; 24-hour bakeries serving burek are a standard post-club option.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §10)

[CONTEXT] The grill/street-food and bakery scene has its own home (`belgrade-grill-and-street-food`); here, late-night food is the practical "what's open after the club" note — cross-link for the full guide.

## 8. Foreigner practicalities

[REPORTED] English menus and staff are reliable in touristic areas (Knez Mihailova, Skadarlija, Beton Hala, the riverfront); neighbourhood venues may be Serbian-only with limited English — food keywords or a translation app help.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §12.1)

[REPORTED] Recurring tourist-trap warnings: surprise music charges or higher live-music-night prices at showpiece kafanas; confusion over per-table vs per-person minimum spends on splavovi (confirm before sitting). Frame these as things to know, not deterrents.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §12.3)
