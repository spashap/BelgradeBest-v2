## 1. The wine story

[CONFIRMED] Serbia has had a wine-production renaissance over the last two decades, with quality regions including Fruška Gora, Šumadija, Timok, Tri Morave and Vršac. Belgrade's restaurants and specialist shops now routinely list local and international wines, some specialising in Serbian labels and indigenous grapes.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §4.1)

[REPORTED] A serious wine-bar ecosystem now exists — a Star Wine List Belgrade selection highlighted around 13 wine restaurants and bars in the city, and wine media note that every neighbourhood now has at least one specialist wine retailer or bar (versus a single wine shop at the turn of the century). Counts/selections perishable.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §4.2)

[CONTEXT] This is a listicle covering two durable threads: Serbian wine (where to drink it, the regions) and rakija (the tradition + where to taste it). Discipline — the wine-bar scene is relatively young, so flag durability honestly; lean on the culture and regions (durable) over any single bar.

## 2. Wine bars (durability flagged honestly)

[REPORTED] **IL Grappolo** — a central wine bar (Obilićev venac) with a broad domestic and foreign selection representing all Serbian wine regions, plus brandies and craft beer; operating at least since 2018 with broad coverage. Durable-leaning.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §4.2)

[REPORTED] **Proces Wine Bar** — focused on Serbian wines including amber/skin-contact labels, knowledgeable staff, social atmosphere; consistently cited by wine media but still relatively young. Durable-leaning.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §4.2)

[REPORTED] **Naše Vino** and **Wine bar Passage** — Serbian-wine-focused bar/shop and a small tasting-room-style bar respectively; strong early reputations but limited long-term track records. Perishable-leaning — name as examples, flag accordingly.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §4.2)

[REPORTED] Many serious restaurants (e.g. Legat 1903, Langouste, Comunale, Iva) double as wine venues showcasing Serbian regions and may be the most practical introduction for non-specialists.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §4.2)

## 3. Rakija — the tradition

[CONFIRMED] Rakija is fruit brandy, central to Serbian hospitality — drunk before, during and after meals, with toasts. The iconic variety is šljivovica (plum); apricot, quince, pear, apple and others are common. Serbia is a major plum grower and šljivovica is synonymous with Serbian hospitality.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8; rakija discovery sources)

[REPORTED] Rakija is treated culturally almost as a folk remedy and is deeply tied to social ritual; dedicated rakija bars in the city offer tastings across many varieties and homemade labels, with knowledgeable staff guiding the tasting. Specific bars are perishable — describe the tasting experience, name durable examples sparingly.
(Source: rakija discovery sources; Food & Nightlife research §8)

## 4. Where wine/rakija fits the night

[CONFIRMED] Wine bars and rakija tasting are a distinct, lower-key going-out mode — sit-and-taste rather than club — and overlap with the kafana (rakija) and fine-dining (wine) worlds.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §4, §8)

[REPORTED] Drink-price context: at clubs/splavovi domestic wine bottles have been reported roughly 25–40 EUR and individual drinks a few EUR; wine-bar and rakija-tasting pricing varies by venue (indicative, as of 2025–2026 — check current menus).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.3)
