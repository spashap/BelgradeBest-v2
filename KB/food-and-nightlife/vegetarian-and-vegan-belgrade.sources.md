## 1. The landscape

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade is a meat-centred food city, but vegetarian and vegan dining has improved over the last decade — a small cluster of dedicated venues plus broad menu integration at mainstream and fine-dining restaurants, which increasingly mark vegetarian dishes and accommodate requests.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.1, §3.2)

[CONTEXT] This page addresses a real foreigner concern (veg/vegan visitors historically struggled here). It is a listicle — name the durable dedicated venues, flag newer ones, and give the practical "how to eat veg in a meat city" guidance.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3; leg archetype decision)

## 2. Dedicated vegetarian/vegan venues (durable anchors)

[REPORTED] **Radost Fina Kuhinjica** — vegetarian/vegan comfort food (Tex-Mex, burgers, Balkan salads, cakes) in a hidden spot near Kalemegdan; operating since at least the mid-2010s and widely reviewed in travel media. Durable (sustained presence, strong visitor reputation).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.1)

[REPORTED] **Mayka** — a vegetarian restaurant with a large menu and notable seitan dishes; established, though with fewer independent English sources than Radost. Durable-leaning.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.1)

[REPORTED] **Veggessence** — a zero-waste vegetarian place with a daily menu and delivery, praised for value; concept-driven and younger. Perishable-leaning — monitor for continuity.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.1)

[REPORTED] Newer vegan spots (e.g. Rai Urban Vege, Paradise Food) are well-reviewed but based largely on social/forum sources — perishable examples; name as illustrative, flag accordingly.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.1)

## 3. Vegan-friendly mainstream and fine dining

[REPORTED] Many mainstream and New-Balkan/fusion venues (e.g. Iva New Balkan Cuisine, Metropolitan, various Beton Hala restaurants) mark vegetarian dishes and can often accommodate vegan or gluten-free requests; "best vegan" lists are frequently dominated by mainstream venues offering plant-based options rather than fully plant-based restaurants.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.2)

---

## 4. Accidental-veg in traditional food

[REPORTED] Some traditional dishes are accidentally plant-friendly — e.g. prebranac (baked beans), posna (fasting) versions of dishes, salads, certain bakery items — useful fallbacks; but cheese and hidden animal fats are common, so vegetarians should clarify ingredients, especially in salads and baked goods.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.2, §12.2)

---

## 5. Halal, kosher and allergies (practical, brief)

[REPORTED] Explicitly halal Serbian-food venues are rare; many halal options are Middle Eastern or fast-food rather than traditional dishes. Kosher dining is more limited, often relying on self-catering or pre-arranged meals. Allergy handling is ad hoc — few venues advertise formal allergen protocols, so communicate directly with staff and be cautious in small kafanas where cross-contamination is likely (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.3, §12.2)

---

## 6. Practical guidance

[REPORTED] English-speaking vegetarians can now eat well across mid-range and fine-dining venues, but dedicated vegan restaurants are few and centre-clustered; strict vegans should research and reserve ahead (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3.2)
