## 1. What Serbian cuisine is

[CONFIRMED] Serbian cuisine is hearty and meat-forward, built on a mix of Ottoman, Central-European and Balkan influences. Meat, pastry/bread, and local vegetables and dairy dominate; meals are typically long and social.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research; Serbian cuisine sources)

[CONTEXT] This page's job is to explain the cuisine to a foreign visitor — the dishes, the staples, how Serbians eat — not to list restaurants. It is the cultural anchor the where-to-eat pages cross-link back to.
(Source: leg archetype decision)

## 2. The core savoury dishes

[CONFIRMED] **Ćevapi** (ćevapčići): small grilled minced-meat sausages (beef, pork, lamb or a mix), served with flatbread, onions and accompaniments like kajmak and ajvar.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources)

[CONFIRMED] **Pljeskavica**: a large grilled minced-meat patty, the Serbian relative of a burger; the Leskovac style is a well-known spicy variant.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources)

[CONFIRMED] **Karađorđeva šnicla**: a rolled, breaded and fried veal or pork escalope stuffed with kajmak, often served with tartar sauce — a Serbian signature.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources)

[CONFIRMED] **Sarma**: cabbage rolls (fermented/pickled cabbage leaves) stuffed with minced meat and rice, slow-cooked; a winter and special-occasion dish.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources)

[CONFIRMED] **Pečenje**: roasted meat (whole-roasted pork, lamb or goat), central to celebrations such as slava and weddings.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources)

## 3. Staples, spreads and breads

[CONFIRMED] **Kajmak** (a clotted-cream dairy spread) and **ajvar** (a roasted red-pepper relish) are the defining accompaniments to Serbian food. **Urnebes** is a spicy cheese-and-pepper salad/spread from the south.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources)

[CONFIRMED] **Burek**: a flaky filled pastry (meat, cheese and other fillings), a bakery staple eaten with yoghurt; **gibanica** is a cheese-and-egg layered pastry. **Komplet lepinja** is a rich western-Serbian flatbread dish with kajmak, gravy and egg.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources; Food & Nightlife research)

[CONFIRMED] **Shopska salad** (tomato, cucumber, pepper, onion, topped with grated white cheese) is the ubiquitous fresh-vegetable side across the Balkans.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources)

## 4. Sweets

[CONFIRMED] Serbian dessert culture spans Ottoman-influenced sweets (e.g. baklava) and Austro-Hungarian-style cakes and custards (e.g. krempita), served in the poslastičarnica (pastry shop) tradition.
(Source: Serbian cuisine sources; Food & Nightlife research §6)

## 5. Drinks at the table

[CONFIRMED] **Rakija** — fruit brandy, most iconically šljivovica (plum) — is central to Serbian hospitality and table culture, drunk before, during and after meals. **Turkish-style coffee** is the traditional everyday coffee.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8, §9; Serbian cuisine sources)

## 6. How Serbians eat (the culture)

[CONFIRMED] Meals are long and communal; the kafana (traditional tavern) is the emblematic social eating-and-drinking space, and food is often shared from central platters rather than plated individually.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8; Serbian cuisine sources)

[REPORTED] Serbian food is widely described as very affordable by European-capital standards, and portions are generous — framing the city as an underrated, good-value food destination (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §9.3; Serbian cuisine sources)

---

## 7. Practical notes for foreigners

[REPORTED] Vegetarians can eat, but the cuisine is meat-centred and cheese/animal fats are common; some dishes (prebranac/beans, posna/fasting versions, salads) are accidentally plant-friendly — clarify ingredients. Strict vegans should plan ahead.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §3, §12.2)
