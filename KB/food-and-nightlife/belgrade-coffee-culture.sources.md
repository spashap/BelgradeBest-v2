## 1. The coffee story

[REPORTED] Coffee is central to Serbian culture, and Belgrade is widely described as coffee-obsessed — it is also commonly cited as home to one of Europe's earliest coffee houses. Traditional finely-ground unfiltered Turkish-style coffee was long the standard. (Founding/first-coffee-house claims treated as reported.)
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6; coffee discovery sources)

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade now has a substantial specialty-coffee movement alongside the Turkish-coffee tradition — micro-roasters and specialty cafés, concentrated notably in Dorćol, serving espresso-based and pour-over coffee.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6; coffee discovery sources)

[CONTEXT] This is a listicle covering two durable threads: coffee (traditional + specialty) and sweets (poslastičarnica). Discipline — lean on the durable institutions, flag newer/trend cafés as perishable, each venue a checkable fact.

## 2. Traditional vs specialty coffee

[CONFIRMED] Two coffee experiences coexist: traditional Turkish-style coffee (thick, strong, brewed and served in the traditional way, central to kafana and home culture) and the modern specialty scene (single-origin, micro-roasted, espresso/pour-over).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6; coffee discovery sources)

[REPORTED] The specialty scene clusters in Dorćol, with micro-roasters and specialty cafés widely cited; specific named cafés are perishable — describe the cluster and the experience, name durable examples sparingly.
(Source: coffee discovery sources)

## 3. Sweets and the poslastičarnica tradition (durable institutions)

[REPORTED] **Pelivan** — widely described as the oldest pastry shop in Belgrade (and arguably Serbia), operating since 1851. Highly durable (mid-19th-century institution).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6)

[REPORTED] **Specijal** — a family pastry shop in the same spot since 1963, later adding a gelato concept. Durable (six-decade history).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6)

[REPORTED] **Hotel Moskva** pastry shop — famous for the "Moskva šnit" cake, a frequently-cited must-try, integrated into the historic hotel. Durable.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6)

[REPORTED] **Crna Ovca** — a modern artisan ice-cream brand popular across the city and in many dessert guides. Durable-leaning (strong brand, younger than the historic houses).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6)

[REPORTED] Newer dessert venues (doughnut, crêperie, matcha/wagashi-style spots) are widely covered in English-language blogs but are inherently perishable — name as examples only, flag accordingly.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6)

## 4. The cultural texture

[CONFIRMED] Belgrade café culture is an outdoor, social, lingering one — locals sit for long coffee-and-conversation sessions; cafés function as everyday meeting and (for specialty spots) co-working spaces.
(Source: coffee discovery sources; Food & Nightlife research §13)

[REPORTED] A traditional Turkish coffee in an everyday café is commonly reported around 150–300 RSD in non-touristy spots, higher in prime locations; specialty and dessert-shop prices vary widely (indicative, as of 2026 — check current menus).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §6, §9.3)
