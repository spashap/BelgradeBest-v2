## 1. What a kafana is

[CONFIRMED] A kafana is more than a tavern: an all-day social space combining roles of restaurant, café, pub and music venue, rooted historically in Ottoman coffeehouses and later evolving into a venue for eating, drinking and live music.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.1)

[CONFIRMED] Kafanas are characterised by simple wooden interiors, traditional hearty food, long social stays, and often live starogradska (old-town) music played by tamburica or accordion bands.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.1)

[CONTEXT] This page explains the institution to a foreign visitor — what it is, how it differs from a restaurant, the etiquette — rather than ranking venues. It is the cultural home the where-to-eat pages cross-link to.
(Source: leg archetype decision)

## 2. Skadarlija and the historic kafanas

[CONFIRMED] Skadarlija is Belgrade's emblematic bohemian quarter — a cobbled street lined with kafanas offering live tamburica music, traditional dishes and late-night socialising.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.1, §11.1)

[REPORTED] The **"?" (Znak Pitanja)** kafana dates to the 19th century and is widely cited as the oldest in the city — originally an Ottoman-era coffeehouse, now serving traditional dishes in a preserved historic interior. Durable institution; treat the precise founding detail as reported.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.1)

[REPORTED] Skadarlija's showpiece kafanas — **Tri Šešira, Dva Jelena, Mali Vrabac, Velika Skadarlija** and others — are durable, long-established institutions with nightly live music and multilingual menus; they are tourist-facing and priced above neighbourhood averages, best framed as "showpiece" experiences. Names durable; current prices/hours perishable.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.3, §11.1)

## 3. Etiquette and the experience

[CONFIRMED] Kafanas are places to linger — meals last hours, with rounds of food, drink and music. Sharing platters from the centre of the table is common rather than individual plating.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.2)

[REPORTED] Live bands circulate between tables taking song requests; guests are expected to tip musicians directly, usually in cash, and dancing late in the night is not unusual. Rakija is central — meals are often started or punctuated with rounds of fruit brandy and toasts.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.2)

[REPORTED] Smoking indoors is still permitted in many kafanas despite evolving regulations, which can surprise visitors from smoke-free countries (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.2)

## 4. Showpiece vs neighbourhood kafanas

[REPORTED] A useful split for visitors: Skadarlija's kafanas are authentic in food and music but geared to visitors and occasions (showpiece); neighbourhood kafanas across Dorćol, Vračar and outlying districts are more local-oriented — simpler, cheaper, less English, deeper everyday culture. Specific named venues in either category are perishable.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.3)

## 5. Costs and practicalities (perishable — indicative only)

[REPORTED] A full kafana meal for two (shared starters, grilled meat, sides, drinks) at a mid-range central or Skadarlija kafana has commonly been reported around 4,000–8,000 RSD (indicative, as of 2024–2025; triangulated from menus and diner reports — check current menus).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.4)

[REPORTED] Tipping around 5–10% for good service is customary, often by rounding up and usually in cash; musicians are tipped separately and directly (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §8.4, §9.2)

---

## 6. Practical notes for foreigners

[REPORTED] Showpiece kafanas have multilingual menus and English-capable staff; neighbourhood kafanas may be Serbian-only with limited English. Some visitors are surprised by music charges or higher prices on live-music nights at the touristed kafanas — frame these as special experiences rather than everyday dining.
(Source: Food & Nightlife research §12.1, §12.3)
