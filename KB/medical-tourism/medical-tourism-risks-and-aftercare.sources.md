## 2. The core risk categories (documented)

[REPORTED] Authoritative sources (including public-health bodies and clinical literature) document recurring medical-tourism risks: variable regulation and oversight between facilities; infection risk, including exposure to drug-resistant organisms and inadequate infection control in some settings; procedure-related complications; and communication/consent issues from language barriers (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections); CDC/PHIN-type sources via research — Tier a)

[REPORTED] Travel-specific risk: flying soon after surgery raises the risk of blood clots (DVT), and travelling long-haul shortly after a major procedure can strain recovery or disturb healing — recovery timelines should account for safe travel (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections) — Tier a)

## 3. Continuity of care — the central problem

[REPORTED] The most-cited structural risk is fragmented follow-up: care is delivered abroad, complications often appear after the patient returns home, medical records are frequently not transferred between the overseas provider and the home doctor, and home-country clinicians may be limited in treating or unwilling to assume responsibility for a procedure done elsewhere (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections); MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §5 — Tier a)

[REPORTED] Aftercare arrangements vary by provider: some partner with home-country providers or offer remote follow-up for a period; others leave the patient on their own once they return. What aftercare is included — and for how long — should be confirmed before committing (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections) — Tier a)

## 4. The financial-risk dimension

[REPORTED] Standard travel insurance typically does not cover planned procedures or their complications; specialist medical-travel insurance exists. Hidden costs can erode the apparent savings — extended stays, last-minute flight changes, corrective treatment, and the cost of returning for revisions. A "free revision" offer means little if the patient can't afford to travel back (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk/cost sections) — Tier a)

[CONTEXT] The detailed cost picture is owned by the costs page — here, the point is that complications carry financial risk and insurance gaps. Cross-link the costs page.
(Source: leg archetype decision)

## 5. Legal recourse

[REPORTED] Pursuing a malpractice or negligence claim across borders is documented as difficult and expensive — different legal systems, jurisdiction issues, and practical barriers to enforcing any "guarantee" from another country. The realistic level of recourse is far lower than at home (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections); MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §5 — Tier a)

[CONTEXT] This is a legal-reality flag, not legal advice — the reader should seek qualified legal advice for their situation. Do not state Serbian malpractice law as settled here.
(Source: leg profile/honesty decision)

## 6. Planning aftercare (informational, not clinical)

[REPORTED] Independent guidance frames sensible planning: confirm what aftercare and follow-up the provider gives and for how long; keep your home doctor informed and obtain your records/operative details; plan recovery time before travelling home; know warning signs that warrant urgent medical attention and where to seek it; and consider specialist insurance. This is planning information — any medical concern means seeing a qualified doctor (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections) — Tier a)

[CONTEXT] Do NOT provide clinical management instructions (how to treat a complication, what symptoms mean medically). Frame as "plan ahead" and "see a doctor," never as self-management.
(Source: leg profile/no-medical-advice decision)
