## 1. Why vetting matters here

[REPORTED] Independent (non-promotional) sources warn that in medical tourism generally, glossy websites and five-star reviews do not reliably indicate medical safety standards; quality and oversight vary widely even within one country, and clinic-reported success/complication figures are usually unaudited (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections); MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §5 — Tier a)

[CONTEXT] This page's job is to hand the reader an independent vetting method — what to verify, what to ask, what a credible answer looks like — so they can judge a clinic themselves rather than trust marketing. It does not rank or recommend clinics.
(Source: leg profile/honesty + MVP no-named-clinics decision)

## 2. Accreditation — what actually exists (verify per facility)

[CONFLICT] "Internationally accredited" is used loosely in medical-tourism marketing. The reader should distinguish genuine, verifiable accreditation from self-claimed quality language. Real signals include recognised international accreditation (e.g. JCI, ISO, Temos) and national licensing/accreditation via the Serbian authorities (the Agency for Accreditation of Health Care Institutions of Serbia / Ministry of Health). Marketing sources assert quality freely; independent sources stress that accreditation must be checked, not assumed. Where a clinic claims accreditation, the reader should verify it with the issuing body directly.
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §4 (accreditation) — Tier a vs Tier b disagreement)

[REPORTED] Whether any given Belgrade facility holds a specific international accreditation is facility-specific and changes — it must be verified at source for each clinic, as of the time of enquiry. Do not assume Serbian private clinics are internationally accredited by default (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §4 — Tier a/b)

[CONTEXT] State accreditation as a *thing to verify*, never as a settled property of "Belgrade clinics." Naming the issuing bodies is the help; asserting any clinic's status is not this page's place.
(Source: leg honesty decision)

## 3. Verifying the practitioner

[REPORTED] Independent guidance for treatment abroad consistently advises verifying the individual practitioner's qualifications and registration (not just the clinic's brand): confirming the surgeon/dentist is registered with the relevant Serbian professional body, checking training and specialty credentials, and being wary where these can't be substantiated (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk/vetting); MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §5 — Tier a)

## 4. The questions to ask before committing

[REPORTED] Independent sources converge on a set of questions a prospective patient should get clear answers to before booking: what exactly the quoted price includes and excludes; who performs the procedure and their credentials; what infection-control and sterilisation standards the facility follows; what happens if there are complications; what aftercare is provided and for how long; whether follow-up is on-site, remote, or left to the home country; and what avenues of recourse exist if something goes wrong (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections); MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §4, §5 — Tier a)

[CONTEXT] These are durable vetting questions, not Serbia-specific facts — present them as the reader's checklist.
(Source: leg archetype decision)

## 5. Reading marketing critically

[REPORTED] Promotional red flags independent sources highlight: extremely cheap "all-inclusive" packages (cheapness can signal lower standards); "free accommodation"/transfer offers whose terms are vague (clinic apartment vs hotel; how many nights); "best clinic" / "higher quality than the rest of Europe" claims with no independent comparative basis; and headline savings figures that use cherry-picked Western comparators (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §2.1, §2.2, §3; MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (what marketing omits) — Tier a/b)

[CONFLICT] On savings specifically: clinics/facilitators commonly advertise 70–80% savings; more neutral cost-comparison analysis puts the realistic all-in saving nearer 20–60% once travel and extras are included. Present both and say which is the more credible basis (the neutral comparison), never flatten to the marketing figure.
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §3; MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental cost) — Tier b promotional vs Tier b cost-comparison)

## 6. Facilitators / agencies

[REPORTED] Medical-tourism facilitators and agencies arrange packages (treatment + logistics). They can simplify a trip but have a commercial interest in the clinics they place patients with; independent guidance is to treat a facilitator's recommendation as a starting point to verify independently, not as impartial advice (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §4; MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk) — Tier a)
