## 2. The governing law

[REPORTED] Serbia's Law on Biomedically Assisted Fertilization (Zakon o biomedicinski potpomognutoj oplodnji) entered into force in 2017 (Official Gazette RS 40/2017, 113/2017) and is the main statute regulating IVF and assisted reproduction. Treat as the governing framework, but state that specifics are interpreted by clinics and may have evolved — verify the current position (as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §2.4, §4 — Tier a legal-studies sources)

## 3. Eligibility, donation, surrogacy — the contested core (CONFLICT)

[CONFLICT] How far donor IVF is permitted, and for whom, is genuinely contested between sources and must be presented as such, not resolved into a clean rule:
- Tier-a academic/legal analyses state that gamete (egg/sperm) and embryo donation and surrogacy have **historically been banned/restricted** in Serbia, with standard IVF limited to a couple's own eggs and sperm.
- Tier-b clinic/aggregator marketing variously claims only own-egg IVF is allowed, OR markets egg-donation IVF (e.g. using pre-frozen oocytes ordered from a Spanish bank, recipients up to a stated age) — which may rely on cross-border donor material and may not be fully aligned with the statute for all patients.
- A 2022 Ministry of Health pilot allowed import of frozen donor gametes from a Spanish tissue bank, with implantation funded by the state insurance fund but gamete-acquisition costs paid by the couple — signalling a gradual relaxation, but specific to Serbian citizens in the public programme, not a general foreigner entitlement.
Present all of this as unresolved: treat Serbia as primarily an OWN-GAMETE IVF destination for foreigners; describe donor programmes as evolving, legally complex, and often limited to citizens in the public scheme; and explicitly advise foreign patients to obtain independent legal advice before relying on any donor programme a private clinic offers. Do not flatten to either the academic "banned" or the clinic "available" version.
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §2.4, §4 — Tier a academic/legal vs Tier b clinic marketing, explicitly disagreeing)

[REPORTED] On age: tier-b sources suggest no statutory upper age limit for IVF using own eggs, though clinics may set their own limits, and some egg-recipient programmes state an upper age. Treat as clinic-dependent and verify (as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §2.4 — Tier b, non-official interpretation)

## 4. Surrogacy — the clearest and highest-stakes legal fact

[REPORTED] Legal commentary indicates Serbia treats surrogacy as prohibited and, per a 2023 analysis, as a **criminal offence**, with potential prison sentences cited around 3–10 years for arranging pregnancy for another person. Foreign patients attempting to arrange surrogacy in Serbia risk criminal exposure. State this plainly as a serious legal risk, attributed to legal analysis and as of 2023 — and direct readers to qualified legal advice; do not soften it, and do not present any route around it.
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §2.4 (surrogacy criminal offence) — Tier a legal analysis)

[CONTEXT] This is the page's most important fact to get right and not minimise. It is stated as a legal-risk flag, not legal advice; the reader must seek qualified legal counsel. Do NOT describe any method, workaround, or "but some clinics…" route — that would be both legally and ethically wrong here.
(Source: leg sensitivity/no-advice decision)

## 5. Belgrade cost picture (perishable — Tier-b)

[REPORTED] Belgrade private-clinic IVF/ICSI packages have been listed around 220,000–360,000 RSD per cycle, typically excluding medications and some tests — mid-range European pricing, not a deep discount. Frame IVF in Serbia as mid-priced, not a bargain (indicative, as of 2024–2025; Tier-b price lists — confirm current quotes).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §2.4 — Tier b)

[REPORTED] An honest guide pitches Serbia as a fertility destination for OWN-GAMETE IVF at mid-range cost with short waits and English-speaking private clinics, while being candid that donor and surrogacy options are legally restricted, contested, and evolving — and that a fertility journey involving donors may need to span multiple countries.
