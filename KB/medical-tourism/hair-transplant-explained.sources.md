## 1. What a hair transplant is (tier-a)

[CONFIRMED] A hair transplant moves hair follicles from a donor area (typically the back/sides of the scalp, which are genetically resistant to balding) to thinning or bald areas. The two main methods are FUE (follicular unit extraction — individual follicular units harvested directly) and FUT (follicular unit transplantation / strip method — a strip of scalp removed and dissected into grafts).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (hair transplant, plain-English + techniques) — Tier a)

## 2. FUE vs FUT and what determines results (tier-a)

[REPORTED] FUE leaves minimal linear scarring and is widely marketed; FUT can yield a large number of grafts in one session but leaves a linear donor scar. Graft count needed depends on the area and degree of loss. Results depend heavily on the patient's donor density and extent of balding — not on clinic marketing alone — and final results take many months to mature (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (hair transplant, techniques + what determines results) — Tier a)

[REPORTED] The multi-session reality: a single session may not achieve the desired density, and a second session is common; ongoing medication may still be advised to manage continued native-hair loss. This is a key expectation marketing downplays (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (hair transplant, repeat-session reality) — Tier a)

## 3. Process, recovery, risks (tier-a)

[REPORTED] The procedure is typically a long outpatient session under local anaesthesia; recovery involves scabbing/redness in the recipient area for days to a couple of weeks, transplanted hair commonly shedding before regrowth, and donor-area healing. Risks include infection, poor graft survival, unnatural-looking results if poorly planned, and donor-area scarring (linear with FUT). Outcomes are operator- and planning-dependent (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (hair transplant, process/risks) — Tier a)

## 4. International cost comparison (perishable — Tier-b)

[REPORTED] Hair-transplant pricing is often per-graft or per-session and varies widely by country. Turkey is the high-volume low-cost leader; Western Europe and Australia are substantially higher; Serbia and similar markets sit mid-price. Cost comparisons consistently group Serbia among mid-price destinations rather than the cheapest tier (indicative, as of 2026; Tier-b cost-comparison data).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (hair transplant, price comparison) — Tier b)

[REPORTED] "All-inclusive" packages commonly exclude repeat sessions, medication, PRP and long-term follow-up — the headline price is rarely the lifetime cost of managing hair loss (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (hair transplant, what marketing omits) — Tier a/b)

## 5. What marketing omits (the honest core — tier-a)

[REPORTED] The biggest misunderstanding: a transplant redistributes existing donor hair, it doesn't create new hair, so results are bounded by donor supply; native hair can keep thinning around transplanted grafts, often requiring ongoing medication and sometimes future sessions. Before/after marketing implies a one-time permanent fix more often than the biology supports (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (hair transplant, what marketing omits) — Tier a)
