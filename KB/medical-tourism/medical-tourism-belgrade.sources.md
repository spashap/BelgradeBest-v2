## 1. What it is and the scale

[REPORTED] Serbia receives an estimated 40,000–60,000 foreign patients per year for medical and dental treatment, according to sector and professional-association estimates (Serbian Chamber of Commerce / industry figures, not an official registry). Treat as an indicative estimate, as of 2025.
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §1, §2.1; Serbian Monitor 2025; NIN 2025 — Tier a)

[REPORTED] Dental work is the dominant category — sector estimates attribute up to roughly 80% of foreign health-tourism demand to dental services — followed by cosmetic/plastic surgery, with fertility, orthopedic and other fields also represented (indicative, as of 2025).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §1, §2.1 — Tier a estimates)

[CONTEXT] This anchor's job is to orient a prospective foreign patient honestly — what the market is, who comes, why, what Serbia is genuinely good at, and what to be careful about — then send them to the dedicated pages. It is not a sales pitch and not a clinic directory.
(Source: leg archetype + profile decision)

## 2. Who comes, and why

[REPORTED] The main inbound patients are the Serbian diaspora and Western Europeans — commonly cited source countries include Germany, Switzerland, Austria, the USA and Australia, plus the UK — drawn by lower cost, shorter waits, and English-speaking private clinics (indicative, as of 2025).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §6, §1 — Tier a/b)

[REPORTED] The realistic motivations are cost savings, avoiding home-country waiting lists, and access to treatment; many patients combine treatment with a visit. Savings are real but commonly overstated by marketing (see §3).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §6 — Tier a/b)

## 3. The savings reality (honest framing — perishable)

[REPORTED] Independent and semi-official sources cite average savings of roughly 50–70% versus Western Europe and the US for common procedures; however, these are not based on systematic peer-reviewed comparison, and clinics tend to cherry-pick high Western comparators (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §2.1, §3 — Tier a estimate + Tier b promotional)

[REPORTED] More neutral cost-comparison analysis suggests that once flights, accommodation, home-country scans and medications are added, the realistic saving on a full course of treatment is closer to 20–60% depending on procedure and country — not the headline 70–80% often advertised (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental cost section) — Tier b cost-comparison)

[CONTEXT] The honest savings message is the page's value: real savings exist, but the headline percentages are marketing, and the all-in figure after travel and extras is lower. The costs page covers this in detail — cross-link it.
(Source: leg honesty/profile decision)

## 4. Which procedures Serbia is genuinely competitive in

[REPORTED] The procedures with the strongest evidence of genuine competitiveness and foreign demand are dental work, cosmetic/plastic surgery, gender-affirming/reconstructive surgery (a distinctive Belgrade niche), fertility/IVF, eye surgery, and elective orthopedics. Cardiology, oncology and bariatric care are present but not leading destinations — for some advanced treatments, Serbian patients themselves travel abroad (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §1, §2 — Tier a/b)

[CONTEXT] Each of these has its own guide; this anchor names them and cross-links. Do not turn this page into per-procedure detail — that's the procedure pages' job.

## 5. The honest cautions

[REPORTED] Independent (non-promotional) sources caution that quality varies between facilities, regulation and accreditation are uneven, complication-handling and continuity of care after returning home are real challenges, and clinic-reported success/complication figures are usually unaudited. A responsible decision means vetting the clinic, planning aftercare, and reading marketing critically (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §5; MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (risk sections) — Tier a)

[CONTEXT] The vetting and the risks/aftercare each have dedicated pages — orient here, cross-link for the detail.
(Source: leg archetype decision)

## 6. Where care clusters

[REPORTED] Private medical and dental clinics serving foreign patients cluster in Belgrade, with Vračar repeatedly cited as the private-healthcare district; Novi Sad and Niš are also mentioned. Belgrade Nikola Tesla Airport is the main international gateway (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalResearch-Belgrade.md §1, §4 — Tier a/b)

[CONTEXT] District identity (what Vračar is as a place) is owned by Visit Belgrade — name it as the clinic cluster only, cross-link for character.
