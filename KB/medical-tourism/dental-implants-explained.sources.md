## 1. What dental implants are (tier-a)

[CONFIRMED] Dental implants are artificial tooth roots (usually titanium) surgically placed in the jawbone to support crowns, bridges or full-arch prostheses, used to replace one or more missing teeth when dentures or bridges aren't sufficient or tolerated.
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, plain-English) — Tier a)

## 2. Techniques and variants (tier-a)

[CONFIRMED] Main variants: single implant + crown (one implant, one tooth); implant-supported bridges (several implants support a multi-tooth bridge); full-arch "All-on-X" (e.g. All-on-4/6) where 4–8 angled implants support a full fixed arch, sometimes immediately loaded; and crowns/veneers as restorative/cosmetic components.
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, techniques) — Tier a)

[CONFIRMED] Cost and complexity are driven by implant number and brand, the need for bone graft or sinus lift, prosthetic material (e.g. zirconia vs acrylic), and whether treatment is staged or immediately loaded.
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental) — Tier a)

## 3. The process and recovery (tier-a)

[CONFIRMED] Implant treatment usually involves diagnostic imaging, possible extractions/bone graft, implant placement under local anaesthesia, a healing period of several months for osseointegration, then abutment placement and the final crown/bridge. Mild-to-moderate pain, swelling and bruising for several days is typical; stitches out around a week; full function delayed until osseointegration and restoration complete.
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, process) — Tier a)

[REPORTED] Full-arch (All-on-4-style) cases involve longer surgery, more swelling and a staged approach (temporary then definitive bridge months later), with biologic complication rates reported around 18.9–25.5% and mechanical complications around 7.3–36.7% despite high long-term implant survival (indicative figures, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, All-on-4) — Tier a)

## 4. Risks and complications (tier-a — faithful)

[REPORTED] Documented complications: in one clinical series (150 cases), infection ~15.3%, peri-implantitis ~12%, implant failure ~8%, with about two-thirds having no complications; large datasets suggest early implant failure around 2–3% overall, higher for short/narrow implants and poor-quality bone; All-on-4 literature reports implant/prosthetic survival ~91.9–99.6% but substantial biologic/mechanical complication rates. Serious but less common: nerve injury, sinus perforation, chronic pain, late peri-implant bone loss (indicative figures, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, risks) — Tier a)

## 5. Candidacy (tier-a)

[CONFIRMED] Good candidates generally have adequate jawbone (or are graftable), good oral hygiene, and controlled systemic conditions. Higher-risk or relative contraindications include heavy smoking, uncontrolled diabetes, severe periodontal disease, untreated bruxism, and inability to maintain hygiene or attend follow-up.
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, candidacy) — Tier a)

## 6. International cost comparison (perishable — Tier-b)

[REPORTED] Indicative single-implant (implant + abutment + crown) prices: USA ~$3,000–$6,000/tooth; UK ~£1,850–£3,000; Germany ~€1,600–€2,700 all-in; Australia ~A$3,000–$7,500/tooth; lower-cost European hubs (Turkey, Hungary, Poland) substantially less. Industry comparisons suggest UK/US pricing is often 2–4× higher than Turkey/Poland/Hungary for full-arch and ~50–70% higher for single implants (indicative, as of 2025–2026; Tier-b cost-comparison data).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, price comparison) — Tier b)

[REPORTED] Marketing often quotes 70–80% headline savings, but neutral comparison platforms note the realistic all-in saving on a full course — after flights, accommodation, home scans and medications — is closer to 20–60% depending on destination (indicative, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental cost) — Tier b cost-comparison)

## 7. What marketing omits (the honest core — tier-a)

[REPORTED] The key misunderstanding: marketing equates "implant survival" with "success," but biologic/prosthetic complications (peri-implantitis, screw loosening, prosthetic fracture, soft-tissue problems) are common even when the implant stays in place, especially in All-on-4. Patients also underestimate the lifelong maintenance burden (hygiene, professional checks, possible revisions) and overestimate how "final" implants are vs bridges/dentures (indicative posture, as of 2026).
(Source: MedicalGeneralResearch-2.md (dental, what marketing omits) — Tier a)
